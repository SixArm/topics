# Drivers of AI Value

## Introduction

Every consequential technology investment must answer one question before all others: *Where does the value come from?* For artificial intelligence, the answer is both broader and more nuanced than most executives initially assume. AI is not a single lever—it is a portfolio of capabilities that can generate value across virtually every dimension of business performance. But realising that value requires a disciplined understanding of the mechanisms through which AI creates economic impact, the conditions under which each mechanism operates, and the strategic choices that determine whether returns are incremental or transformative.

This chapter provides that understanding. It offers a comprehensive taxonomy of AI value drivers, frameworks for quantifying and prioritising them, industry-specific value maps, and real-world case studies that ground every concept in practical experience. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to articulate—with precision and credibility—exactly how AI will create value in your enterprise, and why.

## The Five Fundamental Value Drivers

AI creates business value through five interconnected but distinct mechanisms. Understanding each one—and the interplay among them—is the foundation of any credible AI strategy.

### 1. Operational Efficiency

The most immediately measurable source of AI value is efficiency: doing the same work with fewer resources, or doing more work with the same resources. This is where most organisations begin their AI journey, and for good reason—efficiency gains are tangible, quantifiable, and often achievable within months rather than years.

**How AI drives efficiency:**

- **Process automation.** Robotic process automation (RPA) augmented with AI can handle complex, judgment-intensive tasks that traditional automation cannot—invoice processing with anomaly detection, customer onboarding with document verification, supply-chain exception handling.

- **Predictive maintenance.** Machine-learning models that analyse sensor data from industrial equipment can predict failures before they occur, reducing unplanned downtime by 30–50 percent and extending asset life by 15–25 percent. Siemens reported saving over $1.7 billion across its manufacturing operations through predictive maintenance programmes powered by AI.

- **Intelligent resource allocation.** AI-driven scheduling, routing, and capacity-planning systems optimise the deployment of people, machines, and materials. UPS's ORION routing system, which uses AI to optimise delivery routes, saves the company approximately 100 million miles per year—equivalent to $400 million in annual fuel and labour savings.

- **Quality assurance.** Computer-vision systems inspect products on manufacturing lines with accuracy and speed that exceed human inspectors. BMW deploys AI-powered visual inspection across its paint shops, detecting defects that human inspectors miss and reducing rework costs by approximately 30 percent.

- **Back-office acceleration.** AI-powered document processing, contract analysis, and compliance checking compress tasks that previously required days into minutes. JPMorgan's COiN (Contract Intelligence) platform reviews commercial-loan agreements in seconds—work that previously consumed approximately 360,000 hours of lawyer time annually.

**Quantifying efficiency value:**

Efficiency gains are typically measured in three ways:

| Metric | Description | Example |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| Cost reduction | Direct savings from reduced labour, materials, or downtime | $2M annual savings from automated invoice processing |
| Throughput increase | More output from the same resource base | 40% increase in customer-service tickets resolved per agent |
| Cycle-time compression | Faster completion of business processes | Loan approval time reduced from 5 days to 4 hours |

**The efficiency trap:**

A word of caution: efficiency is necessary but not sufficient. Organisations that pursue only efficiency gains from AI leave transformative value on the table. Worse, they risk creating an internal narrative that AI is "just about cutting costs," which undermines the cultural enthusiasm needed for broader adoption. The most effective AI strategies lead with efficiency to build credibility and fund further investment, then expand into revenue, innovation, and experience.

### 2. Revenue Growth

AI's potential to drive top-line growth is where the strategic conversation becomes most compelling—and most complex. Revenue growth from AI takes multiple forms, each with different time horizons and risk profiles.

**Personalisation at scale.**

The single most powerful revenue driver in consumer-facing businesses is AI-driven personalisation. When Netflix uses recommendation algorithms that influence 80 percent of the content watched on the platform, that is not just a nice feature—it is the core mechanism preventing churn in a $33-billion-revenue subscription business. Amazon attributes approximately 35 percent of its revenue to its recommendation engine. Spotify's Discover Weekly playlists, powered by collaborative filtering and natural-language processing, have become the primary mechanism for user engagement and retention.

The principle extends far beyond media. In financial services, AI-driven personalisation of product recommendations can increase cross-sell rates by 20–40 percent. In retail, personalised pricing and promotions powered by demand-forecasting models routinely lift revenue by 5–15 percent.

**Dynamic pricing and yield optimisation.**

AI-powered pricing systems analyse real-time demand signals, competitor behaviour, inventory levels, and customer willingness-to-pay to optimise pricing continuously. Airlines have used revenue-management systems for decades, but modern AI extends this capability to virtually every industry:

- **Hospitality:** Marriott International's AI pricing system adjusts room rates across its 8,000+ properties in real time, contributing to a reported 2–5 percent revenue uplift.
- **Retail:** Zara's parent company Inditex uses AI-driven demand forecasting to optimise pricing and markdowns, reportedly reducing unsold inventory by 15 percent while maintaining revenue.
- **Insurance:** Progressive Insurance uses AI to price policies at the individual level, enabling it to profitably serve customer segments that competitors avoid.

**New product and service creation.**

AI enables entirely new revenue streams that would be impossible without it. Examples include:

- **Data-as-a-service.** John Deere transformed from an equipment manufacturer to a precision-agriculture platform company by combining IoT sensors on its machinery with AI-powered agronomic insights, creating a recurring-revenue subscription business.
- **AI-powered advisory services.** Wealth-management firms like Wealthfront and Betterment built entire businesses on AI-driven portfolio management. Traditional firms like Vanguard and Schwab followed with hybrid advisory models.
- **Predictive products.** Insurance companies are developing usage-based products (pay-per-mile auto insurance, real-time health monitoring) that were technically infeasible before AI.

**Sales effectiveness.**

AI transforms the sales function by prioritising leads, predicting deal outcomes, recommending next-best actions, and automating administrative tasks. Salesforce reports that customers using its Einstein AI features see an average 30 percent increase in lead-conversion rates. Gong's AI-powered conversation analytics platform helps sales teams identify winning behaviours and replicate them across the organisation, with customers reporting 15–25 percent increases in win rates.

### 3. Innovation Acceleration

AI is increasingly a tool for accelerating the pace and reducing the cost of innovation itself—from drug discovery to materials science to software development.

**Drug discovery and development.**

The pharmaceutical industry offers the most dramatic examples. Traditional drug development takes 10–15 years and costs $2–3 billion per approved drug. AI is compressing both timelines and costs:

- **Insilico Medicine** used AI to identify a novel drug target and design a molecule in 18 months—a process that typically takes 4–5 years. The compound entered Phase II clinical trials in 2024.
- **Recursion Pharmaceuticals** uses AI to analyse cellular images at massive scale, identifying drug candidates that human researchers would never have considered. Its platform evaluates billions of potential drug-disease relationships.
- **Moderna** leveraged AI and mRNA technology to develop its COVID-19 vaccine in record time, demonstrating how AI-enabled platform approaches can transform innovation timelines.

**Materials science and engineering.**

Google DeepMind's GNoME (Graph Networks for Materials Exploration) system discovered 2.2 million new crystal structures in 2023—equivalent to 800 years of materials-science research at historical rates. This kind of AI-accelerated discovery has implications for battery technology, semiconductor design, and sustainable materials.

**Software development.**

AI coding assistants like GitHub Copilot are fundamentally changing software-development productivity. GitHub reports that developers using Copilot complete tasks 55 percent faster and write code with fewer defects. At enterprise scale, this translates to accelerated product development, faster time-to-market, and reduced engineering costs.

**Design and creative processes.**

Generative AI is compressing creative cycles in product design, marketing content creation, and architectural planning. Procter & Gamble uses generative AI to accelerate package design, reducing the ideation-to-prototype cycle from weeks to days.

### 4. Customer Experience Transformation

AI enables a fundamental shift in how organisations interact with customers—from reactive and segmented to proactive and individualised.

**Conversational AI and intelligent support.**

Modern AI-powered customer-service systems go far beyond scripted chatbots. They understand context, sentiment, and intent; they can handle complex multi-turn conversations; and they escalate to human agents when appropriate. The results are striking:

- **Klarna** reported that its AI assistant handles two-thirds of customer-service interactions, performing the equivalent work of 700 full-time agents while achieving customer-satisfaction scores equal to or better than human agents.
- **Bank of America's Erica** virtual assistant has handled over 1.5 billion client interactions since its launch, helping customers with account management, budgeting, and financial guidance.

**Proactive service and anticipatory experiences.**

AI enables organisations to anticipate customer needs before they are expressed:

- **Delta Air Lines** uses AI to predict flight disruptions and proactively rebook passengers, often before they are aware of the problem. This transforms a frustration point into a loyalty-building moment.
- **Amazon's anticipatory shipping** patents describe systems that begin moving products toward customers before orders are placed, based on predictive models of purchasing behaviour.

**Hyper-personalised journeys.**

AI enables the creation of individually tailored customer journeys across every touchpoint—web, mobile, email, in-store, call centre. Starbucks' Deep Brew AI platform personalises menu recommendations, marketing messages, and loyalty rewards for each of its 75+ million rewards members, contributing to measurable increases in visit frequency and average transaction value.

**Voice of the customer at scale.**

Natural-language processing enables organisations to analyse every customer interaction—support calls, social-media posts, product reviews, survey responses—to extract insights at a scale and granularity that were previously impossible. This creates a real-time, continuously updated understanding of customer sentiment, unmet needs, and emerging issues.

### 5. Risk Reduction and Resilience

AI strengthens an organisation's ability to identify, assess, mitigate, and respond to risks across the enterprise.

**Fraud detection and prevention.**

AI-powered fraud-detection systems analyse transaction patterns in real time to identify anomalies that rule-based systems miss. Visa's AI systems evaluate approximately 500 million transactions per day, preventing an estimated $25 billion in annual fraud. Mastercard's Decision Intelligence platform uses AI to assess transaction risk in real time, reducing false declines by 50 percent—a critical improvement because false declines cost merchants more than actual fraud.

**Cybersecurity.**

AI-driven security systems detect threats that evade traditional signature-based approaches. Darktrace's Enterprise Immune System uses unsupervised machine learning to identify anomalous behaviour across enterprise networks, detecting threats in their earliest stages. CrowdStrike's Falcon platform uses AI to analyse over one trillion security events per week, providing threat intelligence that improves protection across its entire customer base.

**Regulatory compliance.**

AI automates the monitoring and enforcement of regulatory requirements, reducing both compliance costs and the risk of violations. In financial services, AI-powered surveillance systems monitor trading activity for potential market manipulation. In healthcare, AI systems ensure that clinical documentation meets regulatory standards. In manufacturing, AI-powered environmental-monitoring systems track emissions and waste in real time.

**Supply-chain resilience.**

AI-driven supply-chain risk management systems monitor global events—weather patterns, geopolitical developments, supplier financial health, transportation disruptions—and recommend proactive responses. Unilever uses AI to model supply-chain scenarios and develop contingency plans, reducing the impact of disruptions on product availability.

**Credit and underwriting risk.**

AI models that incorporate alternative data sources and non-linear relationships consistently outperform traditional scoring models. ZestFinance (now Zest AI) demonstrated that AI-driven underwriting models reduce default rates by 20–30 percent while expanding access to credit for underserved populations.

## The AI Value Chain

Understanding individual value drivers is necessary but not sufficient. You also need to understand how they connect and compound. The AI Value Chain describes the sequence of capabilities that transform raw data into business value:

```
Data Assets  →  AI Infrastructure  →  Model Development  →  Deployment & Integration  →  Business Impact
    ↑                                                                                         |
    └─────────────────────── Feedback Loop ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Stage 1: Data Assets

Value begins with data—its quality, completeness, timeliness, and uniqueness. Organisations with proprietary data assets have a structural advantage. The data does not need to be "big"—it needs to be relevant, clean, and accessible.

**Key questions:**
- What proprietary data do we possess that competitors cannot easily replicate?
- How accessible is our data for AI development? (Is it locked in silos, stored in incompatible formats, or subject to quality issues?)
- What external data sources could augment our internal assets?
- Do we have the data-governance infrastructure to ensure quality, lineage, and compliance?

### Stage 2: AI Infrastructure

Infrastructure includes compute resources (cloud, on-premise, edge), development environments, MLOps platforms, and integration middleware. The infrastructure must support experimentation at speed while enabling production deployment at scale.

### Stage 3: Model Development

This stage encompasses the selection, training, fine-tuning, and evaluation of AI models. The key strategic question is increasingly "build, configure, or buy?"—a topic explored in depth in Section 3 of this book.

### Stage 4: Deployment and Integration

The most common failure point. Models that work in development but never reach production—or that reach production but are not integrated into business workflows—generate zero value. Integration with existing systems (ERP, CRM, supply-chain platforms) is where AI value is realised or lost.

### Stage 5: Business Impact

Value materialises when AI outputs inform decisions, automate processes, or create new capabilities that move business metrics. This stage connects directly to the measurement frameworks covered in the next chapter.

### The Feedback Loop

The most important feature of the value chain is the feedback loop. Every AI deployment generates new data—about model performance, user behaviour, business outcomes—that improves future models. This creates a compounding effect: the more you deploy, the better your data becomes, the more accurate your models become, the more value they generate.

## Direct vs. Indirect Value

A critical distinction that many AI strategies overlook is the difference between direct and indirect value creation.

### Direct Value

Direct value is immediately attributable to a specific AI deployment. It shows up in financial statements as increased revenue, reduced cost, or improved margins. Examples include:

- An AI-powered pricing engine that increases revenue by $10 million per year
- An automated document-processing system that eliminates $3 million in annual labour costs
- A fraud-detection model that prevents $50 million in annual losses

Direct value is relatively straightforward to measure and attribute. It is the foundation of most AI business cases.

### Indirect Value

Indirect value is real but harder to attribute to a specific AI deployment. It manifests as improved decision quality, faster organisational learning, enhanced talent attraction, and strengthened competitive positioning. Examples include:

- **Decision quality.** AI-powered analytics that help executives make better strategic decisions—the value is real but difficult to isolate and quantify.
- **Organisational learning.** AI deployments that force improvements in data quality and governance, which benefit the entire organisation beyond the specific AI use case.
- **Talent attraction.** A reputation as an AI-forward organisation that attracts higher-calibre technical and business talent.
- **Option value.** AI capabilities that do not generate immediate returns but position the organisation to capture future opportunities. Building a recommendation engine, for example, creates a capability platform that can be applied across multiple products and channels.
- **Competitive deterrence.** AI investments that raise the barrier to entry for competitors, even if the specific deployment has modest direct returns.

### Balancing Direct and Indirect Value

The most effective AI strategies pursue a portfolio approach:

| Portfolio Allocation | Time Horizon | Value Type | Risk Profile |
|---------------------|--------------|------------|-------------|
| 50–60% of investment | 0–12 months | Direct, measurable efficiency and revenue gains | Low to moderate |
| 25–35% of investment | 12–36 months | Mixed direct/indirect value from capability building | Moderate |
| 10–20% of investment | 36+ months | Primarily indirect value from transformative bets | High |

This portfolio approach ensures near-term credibility (you can demonstrate ROI quickly), medium-term capability building (you are getting better at AI), and long-term competitive positioning (you are making bets that could be transformative).

## Short-Term vs. Long-Term Value Drivers

### Short-Term Value Drivers (0–12 Months)

Short-term value drivers are characterised by clear ROI, bounded scope, and limited organisational change requirements:

1. **Process automation of high-volume, rule-intensive tasks.** Invoice processing, claims adjudication, customer-inquiry routing. These deliver measurable savings quickly and build organisational confidence.

2. **Predictive analytics on existing structured data.** Demand forecasting, customer-churn prediction, equipment-failure prediction. These leverage data assets that already exist and deliver insights that directly improve decisions.

3. **AI-augmented employee productivity.** Coding assistants, writing tools, meeting summarisation, email triage. These deliver immediate time savings across large employee populations.

4. **Enhanced search and knowledge retrieval.** Enterprise search powered by large language models, customer-facing FAQ systems, internal knowledge bases with conversational interfaces. These reduce friction and improve self-service rates.

### Long-Term Value Drivers (12–36+ Months)

Long-term value drivers require sustained investment, organisational transformation, and patience—but they generate disproportionate returns:

1. **Data flywheels.** Systems where each customer interaction, transaction, or operational event generates data that improves the AI, which improves the product or service, which attracts more customers, which generates more data. (Explored in detail below.)

2. **AI-native products and services.** Products that could not exist without AI—autonomous vehicles, AI-powered drug discovery platforms, real-time language translation services. These create new revenue streams and potentially new markets.

3. **Organisational intelligence.** The cumulative effect of embedding AI into decision-making across the enterprise—from pricing to hiring to capital allocation. Over time, the organisation makes systematically better decisions than competitors who rely on intuition and historical rules of thumb.

4. **Ecosystem effects.** AI capabilities that create value for partners, suppliers, and customers, strengthening the entire ecosystem and raising switching costs.

## Network Effects and Data Flywheels

Among the most powerful long-term value drivers are network effects and data flywheels. These mechanisms create compounding returns that are extremely difficult for competitors to replicate.

### The Data Flywheel

A data flywheel exists when:

1. More users or transactions generate more data
2. More data enables better AI models
3. Better models create a better product or service
4. A better product attracts more users or transactions
5. The cycle repeats, with each revolution improving performance

**Classic example: Google Search.**

Google's search engine is the canonical data flywheel. Billions of daily searches generate data about user intent and satisfaction. This data trains better ranking models. Better rankings attract more users. More users generate more data. After two decades, Google's flywheel has accumulated a data advantage that no competitor has been able to overcome.

**Enterprise example: Shopify.**

Shopify's platform powers millions of merchants. Every transaction across its ecosystem generates data about consumer behaviour, product demand, and pricing dynamics. Shopify uses this data to power AI features—demand forecasting, fraud detection, marketing optimisation—that make its merchants more successful. More successful merchants attract other merchants to the platform, generating more data.

**Building your own flywheel:**

To design a data flywheel, answer these questions:

1. What is the core interaction in our business that generates data?
2. How can AI improve that interaction using the data generated?
3. Does the improvement increase the volume or quality of future interactions?
4. Is the data proprietary—can competitors access the same data?

If you can answer all four affirmatively, you have the foundation for a data flywheel.

### Network Effects in AI

Network effects in AI take several forms:

- **Direct network effects:** Each additional user makes the AI better for all users (e.g., Waze's traffic predictions improve as more drivers contribute data).
- **Indirect network effects:** AI-powered platforms that connect multiple sides of a market (e.g., Uber's matching algorithm improves as both driver and rider populations grow).
- **Data network effects:** The specific case where more data from more users improves the AI, which benefits all users. This is the most common form of network effect in AI-powered businesses.

## Competitive Moats Through AI

AI can create durable competitive advantages—moats—that are difficult for competitors to replicate. Understanding these moats is essential for long-term strategic planning.

### Types of AI Moats

**1. Proprietary Data Moats**

The most durable AI moat is proprietary data that competitors cannot easily acquire. Bloomberg's financial-data terminal, built over four decades of relationships with financial institutions, provides training data for AI models that no startup can replicate. Tesla's fleet of millions of vehicles collecting real-world driving data gives it an autonomous-driving dataset that competitors must spend billions to approximate.

**2. Talent Moats**

Organisations that attract and retain the best AI talent develop compounding advantages—better models, faster iteration, deeper institutional knowledge. Google DeepMind, OpenAI, and Anthropic maintain talent moats that translate directly into research leadership.

**3. Infrastructure Moats**

Purpose-built AI infrastructure—custom chips (Google TPUs, Amazon Inferentia), proprietary training pipelines, specialised deployment systems—creates advantages that are expensive and time-consuming to replicate.

**4. Integration Moats**

AI systems that are deeply integrated into business workflows and customer processes create high switching costs. Palantir's deep integration into government and enterprise decision-making workflows makes it extraordinarily difficult for customers to switch, even if a technically superior alternative exists.

**5. Regulatory Moats**

In regulated industries, AI systems that have received regulatory approval (FDA clearance for medical devices, regulatory certification for financial models) create barriers that new entrants must invest significant time and capital to overcome.

### The AI Value Matrix

A practical tool for mapping AI opportunities across your enterprise:

```
                        High Strategic Impact
                              │
                              │
        Transformative        │        Strategic
        Bets                  │        Imperatives
        (High impact,         │        (High impact,
         high difficulty)     │         achievable)
                              │
    ──────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────
                              │
        Exploratory           │        Quick Wins
        (Low impact,          │        (High impact,
         high difficulty)     │         easy to implement)
                              │
                              │
                        Low Strategic Impact

    High Implementation              Low Implementation
    Difficulty                       Difficulty
```

**How to use this framework:**

1. List all potential AI use cases across your enterprise.
2. Score each on two dimensions: strategic impact (contribution to key business objectives) and implementation difficulty (technical complexity, data requirements, organisational change needed).
3. Plot each use case on the matrix.
4. Prioritise: Start with Quick Wins to build momentum and credibility. Invest steadily in Strategic Imperatives. Fund Transformative Bets selectively. Deprioritise or eliminate Exploratory projects that are hard to execute and have limited impact.

## Quantifying AI Value: Speaking the CFO's Language

One of the CAIO's most important responsibilities is translating AI value into financial language that CFOs, boards, and investors understand. Here are the key frameworks:

### Net Present Value (NPV)

Calculate the present value of future cash flows generated by AI investments, discounted at the company's cost of capital. This is the gold standard for investment decisions.

**AI-specific considerations:**
- Include a ramp-up period (AI projects typically take 6–18 months to reach full value)
- Model the learning curve (AI systems improve over time, so value accelerates)
- Account for data-flywheel effects (compounding returns from improved data)
- Include ongoing costs (model maintenance, retraining, infrastructure, talent)

### Return on AI Investment (ROAI)

A variant of ROI specifically adapted for AI:

```
ROAI = (Value Generated by AI - Total Cost of AI) / Total Cost of AI
```

**Components of value generated:**
- Direct revenue attributable to AI
- Cost savings from AI-driven automation and optimisation
- Risk-adjusted value of avoided losses (fraud, compliance violations, downtime)
- Quantifiable improvements in customer metrics (NPS, retention, lifetime value)

**Components of total cost:**
- Infrastructure (cloud compute, storage, networking)
- Talent (data scientists, ML engineers, AI product managers)
- Data (acquisition, cleaning, storage, governance)
- Tools and platforms (MLOps, development environments, monitoring)
- Organisational change (training, process redesign, change management)

### Value at Risk Without AI

A powerful framing for boards: what is the cost of *not* investing in AI? This includes:

- **Revenue at risk** from competitors who are using AI to personalise, price, and innovate faster
- **Cost disadvantage** from competitors who are automating at a faster rate
- **Talent risk** from being perceived as a technology laggard
- **Regulatory risk** from manual compliance processes that cannot keep pace with evolving requirements

This framing is particularly effective with risk-averse boards that are resistant to AI investment—it reframes the conversation from "should we invest?" to "can we afford not to?"

## Building Your AI Value Thesis

Every CAIO needs a clear, compelling AI value thesis—a concise articulation of how AI will create value for the specific enterprise. Here is a template:

### The AI Value Thesis Template

**1. Value context (one paragraph)**
Describe the business environment, competitive dynamics, and strategic priorities that make AI relevant.

**2. Primary value drivers (three to five bullets)**
Identify the specific value drivers that are most important for your enterprise, with estimated magnitudes.

**3. Strategic differentiators (two to three bullets)**
Articulate what makes your AI opportunity unique—proprietary data, industry position, talent, partnerships.

**4. Value timeline (table)**
Map expected value realisation across three horizons: 0–12 months, 12–36 months, 36+ months.

**5. Investment requirements (summary)**
Outline the investment needed in technology, talent, data, and organisational change.

**6. Risk factors (three to five bullets)**
Identify the key risks that could prevent value realisation and the mitigations in place.

### Example: AI Value Thesis for a Mid-Size Regional Bank

> **Value context:** Our bank serves 2.5 million customers across eight states in an increasingly competitive market. Digital-native challengers are gaining share through superior customer experiences and lower cost structures. ## Common Mistakes in AI Value Assessment

### 1. Confusing Technical Capability with Business Value

An AI model with 99 percent accuracy is worthless if it is solving a problem nobody cares about. Always start with the business problem, not the technical solution.

### 2. Ignoring Integration Costs

The model is typically 10–20 percent of the total cost of an AI deployment. Integration with existing systems, process redesign, change management, and ongoing maintenance account for the rest. Business cases that underestimate these costs overstate returns.

### 3. Assuming Linear Value Growth

AI value typically follows an S-curve: slow initial progress as data is prepared and models are developed, rapid acceleration as models improve and adoption increases, then gradual flattening as optimisation opportunities are exhausted. Business cases that assume linear growth from day one will disappoint.

### 4. Overlooking Organisational Readiness

The most sophisticated AI model deployed into an organisation that is not ready to use it generates zero value. Readiness assessment (covered in Chapter 3) must be part of every value calculation.

### 5. Measuring Activity Instead of Impact

Number of models deployed, number of data scientists hired, and amount of data processed are activity metrics. They say nothing about whether AI is creating value. Focus on business-outcome metrics: revenue generated, costs saved, risks mitigated, customers retained.

### 6. Failing to Account for Compounding

AI value compounds over time through data flywheels, organisational learning, and capability building. Business cases that only model the first year of value significantly underestimate long-term returns.

## Key Takeaways

1. **AI creates value through five fundamental mechanisms:** operational efficiency, revenue growth, innovation acceleration, customer-experience transformation, and risk reduction. A complete AI strategy addresses all five.

2. **The AI Value Chain—from data to deployment to impact—must be managed end to end.** Breakdowns at any stage (especially deployment and integration) destroy value.

3. **Direct value is easier to measure; indirect value is often more important.** Build business cases around direct value, but invest for indirect value (capability building, data flywheels, competitive moats).

4. **Data flywheels and network effects create the most durable competitive advantages from AI.** Identify and invest in them early.

5. **Industry context matters enormously.** The relative importance of value drivers, the magnitude of opportunities, and the specific applications vary significantly by industry. Generic AI strategies underperform industry-specific ones.

6. **Quantify value in financial language.** NPV, ROAI, and Value at Risk Without AI are the frameworks that resonate with CFOs and boards.

7. **Build a clear AI Value Thesis for your enterprise.** It should be specific enough to guide investment decisions and compelling enough to align the organisation.

8. **Avoid the common traps:** confusing technical capability with business value, ignoring integration costs, assuming linear growth, overlooking organisational readiness, and measuring activity instead of impact.
