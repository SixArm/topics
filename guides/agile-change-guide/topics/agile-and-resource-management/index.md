# Agile and resource management

Agile resource management shifts from assigning individuals to tasks toward bringing work to stable, long-lived teams. The goal is maximizing the flow of value rather than maximizing the busy-ness of individuals.

Traditional management treats people as interchangeable resources moved between projects. Agile takes a different approach: teams stay together to build efficiency and trust, while priorities shift through the product backlog. Capacity-based planning replaces individual hour estimates with team velocity, measuring how much work a team historically completes per sprint. Team members develop T-shaped skills, combining deep specialization with broad competencies so they can help each other and avoid bottlenecks.

Agile applies Theory of Constraints thinking to identify where work piles up. Work-in-progress limits, common in Kanban, cap how many items can be active simultaneously, forcing teams to finish current work before starting new work. This prevents resource spread, where everyone stays busy but nothing reaches completion. When high-priority items get blocked, teams swarm the problem regardless of individual roles.

At the enterprise level, resource management becomes a strategic balancing act. Portfolio Kanban ensures organizations do not start more initiatives than their teams can finish. Cost of delay guides allocation decisions, directing capacity toward work that loses the most value when postponed.

Agile prioritizes throughput over utilization. Running teams at full capacity actually slows delivery because there is no slack to absorb unexpected problems or urgent requests. Sustainable pace and team health are treated as resource management concerns, since high turnover represents one of the largest hidden costs in any organization.
