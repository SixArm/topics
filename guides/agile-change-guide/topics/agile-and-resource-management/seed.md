# Agile and resource management

In Agile, resource management shifts from "assigning people to tasks" to "bringing work to stable teams." The goal is to maximize the flow of value rather than the busy-ness of individuals.

## 1. From Individual Tasks to Stable Teams

Traditional management treats people like interchangeable "resources" moved between projects. Agile focuses on Long-Lived Teams:

Capacity-Based Planning: Instead of estimating hours for individuals, teams plan based on their known Velocity (how much work they historically complete in a sprint).

Fixed Teams, Variable Scope: The team stays together to build "social capital" and efficiency. If priorities shift, the Product Backlog changes, but the team structure remains intact.

T-Shaped Skills: Agile encourages members to have a deep specialty (the vertical bar of the T) but also a broad set of basic skills (the horizontal bar) so they can help each other and avoid bottlenecks.

## 2. Managing the "Bottleneck" (Constraints)

Agile resource management often uses Theory of Constraints to identify where work is piling up:

WIP Limits (Work in Progress): In Kanban, teams limit how many items can be "in progress" at once. This forces the team to finish current tasks before starting new ones, preventing "resource spread" where everyone is busy but nothing gets done.

Swarming: When a high-priority item is blocked, the "resources" (team members) swarm it, regardless of their specific role, to get it across the finish line.

## 3. Lean Resource Governance

At the enterprise level, resource management becomes a strategic balancing act:

Demand vs. Capacity: Organizations use Portfolio Kanban to ensure they aren't starting more projects than their teams have the capacity to finish.

The "Cost of Delay": Resources are allocated based on which initiatives lose the most value if they are delayed, rather than who "shouts the loudest."

Key Metrics for Resource Health

Utilization vs. Throughput: Agile prioritizes Throughput (items finished) over Utilization (percentage of time spent working). High utilization (100% busy) actually slows down delivery because there is no "slack" to handle unexpected problems.

Team Happiness/Morale: High turnover is a massive resource risk; Agile tracks team health to ensure sustainable pace and retention.
