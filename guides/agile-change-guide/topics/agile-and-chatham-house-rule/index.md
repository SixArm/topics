# Agile and the Chatham House Rule

Agile teams thrive on open, honest communication. Planning sessions, reflection
discussions, and cross-functional workshops all depend on participants sharing
candid feedback without fear of blame or professional consequences.

The Chatham House Rule states that participants may use information shared in a
meeting, but may not reveal the identity or affiliation of any speaker. This
simple agreement unlocks frank discussion among people who might otherwise hedge
their words due to corporate policy, competitive dynamics, or political
exposure.

Organizations also apply the pairing when running agile policy-making workshops,
where iterative, stakeholder-driven processes benefit from participants who feel
free to challenge assumptions and surface inconvenient truths. Government and
programs have adopted similar principles for evidence-based strategy development
conducted in closed sessions.
