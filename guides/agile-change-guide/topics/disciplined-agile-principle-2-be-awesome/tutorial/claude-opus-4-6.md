# Disciplined Agile principle 2: Be awesome

The second principle of Disciplined Agile states: "Build high-performing, motivated teams where individuals are supported to be their best and continuously improve." This is the people-first principle of the Disciplined Agile toolkit, recognizing that agile success depends not merely on processes, frameworks, or tooling, but on the humans who execute them. Where many agile approaches focus primarily on ceremonies and artifacts, Disciplined Agile elevates people to the center of the conversation. For technology professionals, this principle serves as a persistent reminder that sustainable delivery requires investing in the growth, safety, and motivation of every team member.

## Psychological safety as a foundation

Being awesome starts with creating workplaces where people thrive, and the prerequisite for thriving is psychological safety. Organizations must establish environments where team members feel comfortable taking risks, admitting mistakes, and raising concerns without fear of retribution. When people feel safe, they experiment more freely, surface problems early, and challenge assumptions constructively. This directly improves delivery outcomes because defects are caught sooner, technical debt is discussed openly, and retrospectives produce genuine insights rather than performative agreement.

Psychological safety is not a vague aspiration. It manifests in concrete behaviors:

- Leaders respond to mistakes with curiosity rather than blame.
- Team members ask questions without fear of appearing incompetent.
- Dissenting opinions are welcomed during design reviews and planning sessions.
- Postmortems focus on systemic causes rather than individual fault.
- People voluntarily share early-stage, incomplete work for feedback.

Without psychological safety, every other investment in team performance yields diminished returns. A team with excellent tooling but a culture of fear will underperform a team with modest tooling but genuine trust.

## Developing T-shaped professionals

The principle calls for developing T-shaped individuals who combine deep expertise in one discipline with enough breadth to collaborate effectively across the team. This model directly addresses several persistent problems in software delivery.

| Shape | Description | Strength | Weakness |
|-------|-------------|----------|----------|
| I-shaped | Deep specialist in one area only | High expertise in a single domain | Creates bottlenecks; cannot cover for others |
| T-shaped | Deep in one area, broad across others | Flexible collaboration with reduced bottlenecks | Requires sustained investment in learning |
| E-shaped | Deep in multiple areas, broad across others | Highly versatile and resilient | Rare; takes years to develop |

For technology teams, T-shaped development means that a backend engineer understands enough about frontend concerns to write meaningful API contracts, a QA specialist can read production logs to triage issues, and a product owner understands deployment pipelines well enough to make informed release decisions. This skill diversity reduces handoff delays, improves knowledge sharing, and makes teams more resilient when priorities shift or members are unavailable.

Organizations foster T-shaped growth through deliberate practices:

- Pair programming and mob programming across discipline boundaries.
- Rotation of responsibilities such as on-call duties and release management.
- Dedicated learning time, including internal tech talks and structured mentorship.
- Stretch assignments that expose individuals to adjacent domains.

## Continuous improvement and the Kaizen mindset

Continuous improvement is central to being awesome. Disciplined Agile embraces the Kaizen mindset, giving teams both the time and authority to experiment with their Way of Working. Rather than prescribing a fixed process and expecting compliance, DA encourages teams to evolve their practices based on what they learn through actual delivery.

This principle drives Guided Continuous Improvement (GCI), the DA technique that uses the process goal diagrams and decision points in the toolkit to identify the most promising next steps for team growth. GCI distinguishes DA from frameworks that offer a single prescribed path. Instead, teams evaluate their current context, identify pain points, and select practices that address those specific challenges.

Key aspects of the Kaizen mindset in practice:

- **Small, incremental changes** rather than large process overhauls. A team might experiment with limiting work in progress for two sprints before deciding whether to adopt the practice permanently.
- **Evidence-based decisions** where teams measure outcomes before and after a change, using metrics such as cycle time, defect rates, or team satisfaction scores.
- **Regular reflection cadences** through retrospectives, process tailoring workshops, and periodic health checks.
- **Permission to revert** changes that do not produce the expected improvement, without stigma or bureaucratic overhead.

## Joy at work and intrinsic motivation

Joy at work matters. Motivated people who take pride in their craft produce higher quality results and better business outcomes. This is not sentimentality; it is a practical recognition supported by decades of research that engagement correlates directly with performance, retention, and innovation.

The factors that drive intrinsic motivation in knowledge work are well understood:

| Factor | Description | Example in technology teams |
|--------|-------------|-----------------------------|
| Autonomy | Control over how work is performed | Teams choose their own tools, practices, and technical approaches |
| Mastery | Opportunity to develop and refine skills | Dedicated time for learning, conference attendance, certification support |
| Purpose | Connection to meaningful outcomes | Clear line of sight from daily work to customer value and organizational mission |
| Belonging | Sense of connection to the team | Inclusive team rituals, shared ownership of outcomes, collaborative problem-solving |

When organizations undermine these factors through micromanagement, rigid process mandates, or disconnecting teams from the impact of their work, motivation erodes. The consequence is not just unhappiness but measurable degradation in throughput, quality, and retention. Technology professionals who feel awesome about their work and their team consistently outperform those who are merely compliant.

## Building high-performing teams

High performance is not a state that teams reach once and maintain automatically. It requires ongoing investment and deliberate leadership. The Disciplined Agile toolkit identifies several strategies that support sustained high performance:

- **Whole team ownership**: Everyone on the team shares responsibility for quality, delivery, and continuous improvement. There is no separate group that "owns" quality or process.
- **Stable teams**: Keeping teams together over time allows them to develop shared mental models, efficient communication patterns, and deep trust. Frequent reorganization destroys accumulated team intelligence.
- **Right-sizing governance**: Bureaucratic overhead demoralizes teams and slows delivery. DA advocates for just enough governance to manage risk without stifling autonomy.
- **Servant leadership**: Leaders focus on removing obstacles, securing resources, and shielding teams from organizational noise rather than directing daily work.
- **Celebrating learning**: Teams that celebrate experiments, including failed ones, build a culture where improvement is valued over predictability.

## Practical application for technology professionals

For technology professionals, this principle reinforces that investing in team culture, individual growth, and working conditions is not optional overhead. It is a core driver of sustainable, high-quality delivery. Applying this principle involves concrete actions at every level:

- **Individual contributors** take ownership of their own growth, seek feedback actively, and contribute to a psychologically safe environment by being respectful and curious.
- **Tech leads and architects** mentor others, share context broadly, and design systems that distribute knowledge rather than concentrating it.
- **Engineering managers** protect team stability, advocate for learning time, measure team health alongside delivery metrics, and address toxic behaviors swiftly.
- **Executives** fund professional development, resist the urge to reorganize teams for short-term convenience, and model the vulnerability that psychological safety requires.

The principle also has direct implications for technical practices. Code reviews conducted with respect and curiosity improve both code quality and team trust. Blameless postmortems after incidents strengthen systems and relationships simultaneously. Investing in developer experience through better tooling, faster build times, and reduced toil is an expression of this principle in engineering terms.

## Related

Topics to explore next include the other Disciplined Agile principles, particularly Principle 1 (Delight Customers) which connects team motivation to customer outcomes, and Principle 3 (Pragmatism) which provides the practical lens for deciding how to invest in team improvement. The broader concepts of Guided Continuous Improvement, the DA process goal framework, and the DA tool kit provide the mechanisms for applying this principle systematically. Beyond DA, related topics include psychological safety research by Amy Edmondson, Daniel Pink's work on intrinsic motivation, and the State of DevOps research linking culture to delivery performance.

## Summary

Disciplined Agile Principle 2 asserts that building awesome teams is not a feel-good aspiration but a practical necessity for delivering value sustainably. By establishing psychological safety, developing T-shaped professionals, embracing Kaizen-driven continuous improvement, and nurturing intrinsic motivation through autonomy, mastery, purpose, and belonging, organizations create the conditions for high performance. For technology professionals, this principle demands concrete investment in people, culture, and working conditions as a first-class concern alongside architecture, tooling, and process, because the quality of the team determines the quality of everything the team produces.
