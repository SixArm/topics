# Get out of the building

"Get out of the building" (GOOB) is a principle coined by Steve Blank, a foundational figure in the Lean Startup movement. It challenges the dangerous assumption that teams already know what their customers want. Blank argues that there are no facts inside the building — only opinions. To build products people actually need, teams must leave the office, observe potential users in their real-world environments, and validate business hypotheses through direct interaction. For technology professionals, this principle is a critical counterweight to the tendency to over-engineer solutions before confirming that the problem is real, significant, and worth solving.

## Why It Matters

Most failed products do not fail because of poor engineering. They fail because the team built something nobody wanted. The GOOB philosophy attacks this root cause by insisting that learning precedes building. Inside the building, teams develop assumptions based on their own experiences, internal debates, and competitive analysis. These assumptions feel like facts because they emerge from intelligent discussion — but they remain untested. Outside the building, reality provides feedback that no amount of internal brainstorming can replicate. Customer conversations reveal unexpected workflows, unarticulated pain points, and surprising priorities that reshape product direction.

## Origins and Context

Steve Blank developed the Customer Development methodology in the late 1990s and early 2000s, drawing on his experience founding and advising multiple Silicon Valley startups. He observed that traditional product development assumed a linear path from concept to launch, with customer validation happening only after significant investment. His 2005 book *The Four Steps to the Epiphany* formalized the GOOB approach. Eric Ries later built upon Blank's work to create the Lean Startup methodology, which added the Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop and the concept of the Minimum Viable Product (MVP). Together, these frameworks shifted how technology companies approach uncertainty.

## The Four Phases of Customer Development

Blank's framework structures the GOOB process into four sequential phases, each with distinct objectives and exit criteria:

| Phase | Objective | Key Activity | Exit Criteria |
|-------|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Customer Discovery | Test hypotheses about customer problems | Interviews, observation, problem validation | Evidence that a real problem exists for a defined segment |
| Customer Validation | Prove the business model is repeatable | Early sales, pricing tests, channel experiments | Customers pay for the solution; sales process is documented |
| Customer Creation | Drive end-user demand at scale | Marketing campaigns, demand generation | Scalable acquisition channels identified and tested |
| Company Building | Transition from learning to execution | Formal departments, processes, hiring | Organization structured for growth and operational efficiency |

Technology professionals most commonly engage with the first two phases. Customer Discovery and Customer Validation are where engineering effort is most at risk of being wasted, and where GOOB yields the highest return on time invested.

## How GOOB Integrates with Agile

In a product team, Agile provides the iterative building process, while Customer Development through GOOB provides the "why" and "for whom." These methodologies are complementary, not competing:

- **Customer Development without Agile** produces validated insights but lacks the engineering speed to act on them before the market shifts.
- **Agile without Customer Development** produces rapid iterations but risks optimizing a product that solves the wrong problem.
- **Both together** create a feedback loop where insights from outside the building translate into sprint priorities, and each iteration generates new hypotheses to test externally.

The goal is not to gather data in the abstract or to run focus groups for confirmation. The goal is to gain deep insight into the customer's daily routine, pain points, and decision-making so you can build a Minimum Viable Product that solves a real, validated problem.

## Techniques for Getting Out of the Building

Practitioners use several structured approaches to maximize the value of customer interactions:

- **Problem interviews**: Conversations focused exclusively on understanding the customer's current situation, not pitching a solution. Ask about their workflow, frustrations, and workarounds.
- **Contextual inquiry**: Observing users in their actual work environment to see behaviors they cannot articulate in an interview setting.
- **Solution interviews**: Presenting a prototype or concept to gauge reaction, willingness to pay, and perceived value relative to alternatives.
- **Day-in-the-life observation**: Shadowing a potential user through their routine to identify friction points they have normalized and no longer mention.
- **Concierge testing**: Manually delivering the proposed service to a small number of users to validate demand before building automation.
- **Landing page tests**: Publishing a value proposition and measuring sign-up intent before writing production code.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | How to Avoid It |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| Pitching instead of listening | Founders are passionate about their solution | Structure interviews around the customer's problem, not your product |
| Confirmation bias | Teams seek evidence that supports existing beliefs | Ask disconfirming questions; look for reasons you might be wrong |
| Talking only to friendly contacts | Convenience and comfort | Seek out strangers who match your target persona |
| Treating interviews as focus groups | Misunderstanding the methodology | Have one-on-one conversations; group dynamics distort responses |
| Stopping too early | Pressure to start building | Continue until patterns stabilize and new interviews yield no surprises |
| Delegating all customer contact | Engineers prefer building to talking | Ensure technical team members participate directly in at least some interviews |

## When to Apply GOOB

The principle applies most strongly in conditions of high uncertainty. Not every engineering task requires fresh customer validation, but any significant product decision benefits from external input:

- **Starting a new product or feature**: Validate the problem exists before committing engineering resources.
- **Pivoting direction**: Confirm that the new direction addresses a real need, not just an internal hypothesis.
- **Resolving internal disagreements**: Replace opinion-based debates with evidence from customer conversations.
- **Post-launch stagnation**: When adoption is below expectations, revisit assumptions about who benefits and why.
- **Entering a new market segment**: Existing customer knowledge does not transfer automatically to a new audience.

## Related

Professionals exploring GOOB should also study Lean Startup methodology for the broader Build-Measure-Learn framework, Customer Development for the full four-phase process, Jobs to Be Done theory for understanding customer motivations at a deeper level, Design Thinking for structured empathy and ideation practices, and Minimum Viable Product strategy for deciding what to build first. Understanding personas and user story mapping will also strengthen the connection between customer insights and engineering priorities.

## Summary

"Get out of the building" is a discipline, not a slogan. It requires technology professionals to accept that their assumptions about customers are hypotheses, not facts, and that the only way to reduce risk is through direct engagement with the people they intend to serve. The core lesson is to validate assumptions with real users before investing engineering effort — to ship learning before shipping code. Teams that internalize this principle waste less time building unwanted features, pivot faster when assumptions prove wrong, and ultimately deliver products that solve problems people care enough about to pay for.
