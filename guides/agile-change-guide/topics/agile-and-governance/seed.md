# Agile and governance

In Agile, governance moves away from "command and control" and toward "enablement and transparency." Instead of using heavy upfront planning and manual approvals, Agile governance uses real-time data and frequent checkpoints to ensure the team is delivering value while managing risk. 

## The Shift: Waterfall vs. Agile Governance

Waterfall (Static): Relies on predefined milestones, bulky status reports, and "gatekeeper" sign-offs before moving to the next phase.

Agile (Dynamic): Relies on empirical process control—making decisions based on the actual state of the working software at the end of every sprint. 

## How to Implement Agile Governance

Effective governance in an Agile environment focuses on three main pillars: 

1. Transparency Through Information Radiators
- Rather than waiting for a monthly report, stakeholders have real-time access to the team's progress, such as via task trackers.

2. Lean Budgeting and Guardrails
- Rather than traditional annual budgeting,  
- Value Stream Funding: Funding a team or product line rather than a specific project.
- Participatory Budgeting: Collaborative sessions where stakeholders decide how to allocate resources based on current priorities.
- Guardrails: Pre-defined spending limits or technical standards that teams must stay within to maintain autonomy. 

3. The "Definition of Done" (DoD)
- The DoD acts as a primary governance contract. It is a shared checklist that ensures every piece of work meets the organization's standards for quality, security, and architecture before it can be called "finished."
