# Burndown chart

A burndown chart is an Agile project management tool that visually tracks remaining work against time, displaying a downward trend toward zero. It typically features time on the horizontal axis and remaining effort on the vertical axis, measured in hours or story points. Teams use burndown charts to monitor progress, spot delays, and manage scope creep throughout a sprint or release.

The chart has two primary lines. The ideal work remaining line is a straight diagonal from the total planned effort down to zero, representing the expected steady pace of completion. The actual work remaining line tracks real progress, which typically fluctuates as the team completes tasks, encounters obstacles, or receives new requirements.

Reading the chart is straightforward. When the actual line runs below the ideal line, the team is ahead of schedule. When it runs above, the team is falling behind and may need to adjust scope or capacity. Upward spikes in the actual line indicate that new work was added mid-sprint, making scope creep immediately visible.

Burndown charts provide three core benefits. They allow teams to forecast completion dates based on current velocity. They surface bottlenecks early, giving the team time to course-correct before a sprint ends. They also create transparency for stakeholders by offering a simple, at-a-glance view of whether delivery is on track.

Burndown charts work best when the team updates remaining effort daily and keeps task granularity consistent. They are most commonly used at the sprint level in Scrum but can also track progress across an entire release or project.
