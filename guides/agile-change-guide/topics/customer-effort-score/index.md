# Customer Effort Score (CES)

Customer Effort Score (CES) is a metric that measures how much effort a customer must expend to interact with a company or resolve an issue. Introduced by the Corporate Executive Board (CEB) in 2010, the core philosophy is that reducing customer effort drives loyalty more effectively than attempting to "delight" customers with exceptional experiences.

The standard CES survey asks customers to rate their agreement with a statement such as "The company made it easy for me to handle my issue." This is typically sent immediately after a specific interaction, using a 7-point Likert scale ranging from "Strongly Disagree" to "Strongly Agree." The score is calculated as the average of all individual responses. On a 7-point scale, a score of 5.5 or higher is generally considered good.

CES is a transactional metric triggered by specific events: after a support interaction, after a purchase, during onboarding, or following self-service use. This immediacy captures the experience while it is fresh, producing more accurate signal than relationship-level surveys sent days or weeks later.

The metric matters for three reasons. First, it predicts loyalty: customers who experience low effort are significantly more likely to repurchase. Second, it reduces churn: high-effort interactions are a leading indicator of disloyalty. Third, it lowers operational costs, since low-effort interactions typically involve fewer repeat contacts and escalations.

For technology teams, CES is particularly useful for evaluating onboarding flows, self-service documentation, checkout processes, and support tooling. It provides a direct, actionable signal about where friction exists in the customer experience, enabling teams to prioritize improvements based on measured effort rather than assumptions.
