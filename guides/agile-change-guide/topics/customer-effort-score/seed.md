# Customer Effort Score (CES)

Customer Effort Score (CES) is a metric used to measure how much effort a customer has to put in to interact with a company or resolve an issue. Introduced by the Corporate Executive Board (CEB) in 2010, the core philosophy is that reducing customer effort is a better driver of loyalty than trying to "delight" them.

## Core Survey Question

The modern standard (often called CES 2.0) typically asks customers to rate their agreement with this statement:

- "The company made it easy for me to handle my issue."

## How to Measure and Calculate

CES is usually gathered through a single-question survey sent immediately after a specific interaction.

Survey Scales:

- Likert Scale (1-7): Ranging from "Strongly Disagree" (1) to "Strongly Agree" (7).
- Numbered Scale (1-5 or 1-10): 1 often represents "Very Easy" and 5/10 "Very Difficult" (though some companies invert this).
- Emoticon Scale: Simple faces (Happy, Neutral, Sad) for quick, high-response feedback.

Calculation Formula:

- Average Score: Add up all individual scores and divide by the total number of respondents.

## What is a "Good" Score?

Benchmarks vary by industry and the specific scale used, but general targets include:

- 7-Point Scale: Aim for 5.5 or higher.
- 5-Point Scale: Aim for 4.0 or higher.
- Agreement Percentage: Aim for 80% or more of customers agreeing the interaction was easy.

## When to Use CES

CES is a transactional metric, meaning it should be triggered by specific events while the experience is fresh:

- Post-Support: After a phone call, live chat, or email resolution.
- Post-Purchase: Immediately after checkout to gauge ease of transaction.
- Onboarding: After a customer sets up a new product or account.
- Self-Service: After a customer uses a help centre, FAQ, or chatbot.

## Why it Matters

- Predicts Loyalty: Customers with low-effort interactions are more likely to repurchase, compared to those with high-effort experiences.

- Reduces Churn: High effort is a leading indicator of customer churn; 96% of customers with high-effort interactions report being disloyal.

- Lowers Costs: Low-effort interactions typically cost 37% less than high-effort ones because they involve fewer repeat calls and escalations.
