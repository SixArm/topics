# Agile swarming

Agile swarming is a collaborative technique where multiple team members—often the entire team—work together on a single user story or task to get it to "Done" as quickly as possible.

It is the physical manifestation of the Agile mantra: "Stop starting, start finishing."

## How Swarming Works

Unlike traditional development where individuals work in "silos" on their own tickets, swarming focuses the team’s collective brainpower on a specific bottleneck or high-priority item.

- The Trigger: Swarming usually happens when a high-priority story is blocked, or when a team notices too much Work in Progress (WIP) and needs to clear the board.

- The Roles: Everyone contributes based on their skills. A developer might write code, while a tester simultaneously writes the test scripts, and another team member updates the documentation or clears environmental hurdles.

- The Goal: To reduce Cycle Time (the time it takes for one item to move from start to finish) rather than maximizing individual "busyness."

## The Benefits of Swarming

- Eliminates Hand-offs: By working together, you remove the "waiting time" that occurs when a developer finishes a task and waits for a tester to become available.

- Knowledge Sharing: It’s a natural way to break down "knowledge silos." Junior members learn from seniors, and developers gain insight into testing or ops requirements.

- Team Ownership: It reinforces that the team succeeds or fails together, rather than individuals just completing their assigned tickets.

- Focus on Flow: It prevents the "90% done" syndrome where many tasks are started but none are actually finished and usable.

## Swarming vs. Pairing

While related, they differ in scale:

- Pairing: Two people, one keyboard, one task.

- Swarming: The entire team (or a large portion of it) coordinating on one task or story until it meets the "Definition of Done."
