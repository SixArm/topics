# Agile work-in-progress (WIP)

In Agile, work-in-progress (WIP) refers to any task that has been started but is not yet finished. It represents inventory in your workflow: work that consumes time and energy but has not yet delivered value to the customer. Managing WIP effectively is one of the highest-leverage practices a team can adopt.

In frameworks like Kanban, teams set WIP limits: a fixed cap on the number of items allowed in a specific stage of the workflow, such as allowing only three tasks in testing at a time. These constraints are deliberate and powerful.

Limiting WIP reduces context switching. When team members juggle too many tasks, they lose time re-learning where they left off, and focused attention leads to faster completion. Limiting WIP also exposes bottlenecks. If your "In Progress" column is empty but "Testing" is at its limit, you immediately see where the flow is blocked. According to Little's Law, the more items in progress, the longer any single item takes to complete, so limiting WIP directly shortens lead time. Quality improves as well, because developers focusing on fewer tasks make fewer rushed mistakes.

The guiding mantra is "stop starting, start finishing." Instead of picking up a new task because you are blocked on another, the goal is to resolve the blockage on the current task first.

To calculate WIP, count the items currently in active columns on your board, excluding "To Do" and "Done." Too much WIP produces high stress, slow delivery, and stale code. Too little WIP means idle team members, though this is rare in practice. Watch for common warning signs such as handoff pile-ups at QA, which signal that upstream WIP limits are too high, and ghost WIP where people work on tasks not represented on the board, destroying predictability.
