# Agile pairing

Agile pairing, also known as pair programming, is a practice where two developers work together at a single workstation on the same piece of code. It is a core pillar of Extreme Programming (XP) and is designed to improve code quality, spread knowledge, and solve complex problems faster through continuous real-time review.

In a standard pairing session, the two developers take on distinct roles. The Driver has hands on the keyboard, focusing on writing specific lines of code, syntax, and implementation details. The Navigator acts as an observer and reviewer, watching the bigger picture, checking for logic errors, considering edge cases, and ensuring the code aligns with overall architectural goals. The roles typically switch every 15 to 30 minutes to keep both participants engaged and prevent fatigue.

Two common techniques are strong-style pairing and ping-pong pairing. In strong-style pairing, the Navigator gives instructions and the Driver executes them, which works well for mentoring because it forces the Navigator to clearly articulate their thinking. In ping-pong pairing, used with Test-Driven Development, one developer writes a failing test, the other writes code to make it pass, then writes the next failing test in return.

Pairing delivers several benefits. Two sets of eyes catch errors instantly, often eliminating the need for a separate code review cycle. It is the fastest way to onboard a new team member or teach best practices. It builds resilience by ensuring more than one person understands every piece of code.

Pairing is also high-intensity. It requires constant communication, and most teams sustain it for only four to six hours a day. Success depends on a culture of psychological safety where developers feel comfortable making mistakes in front of each other.
