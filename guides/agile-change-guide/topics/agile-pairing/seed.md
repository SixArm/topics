# Agile pairing

Agile pairing, a.k.a. agile pair programming, is an technical practice where two developers work together at a single workstation on the same piece of code. It is a core pillar of Extreme Programming (XP) and is designed to improve code quality, spread knowledge, and solve complex problems faster through continuous real-time review.

## How it Works: The Two Roles

In a standard pairing session, the two developers take on specific roles:

- The Driver: This person has their hands on the keyboard. They focus on the immediate task—writing the specific lines of code, syntax, and implementation details.

- The Navigator: This person acts as the "observer" or "reviewer." They look at the bigger picture, checking for logic errors, considering edge cases, and ensuring the code aligns with the overall architectural goals.

The roles usually switch frequently (every 15–30 minutes) to keep both participants engaged and prevent fatigue.

## Common Techniques

- Strong-Style Pairing: The Navigator gives instructions, and the Driver executes them. This is excellent for mentoring, as it forces the Navigator to clearly articulate their thoughts.

- Ping-Pong Pairing: This is used with Test-Driven Development (TDD). Developer A writes a failing test; Developer B writes the code to make it pass; Developer B then writes the next failing test for Developer A to solve.

## Why Do It? (The Benefits)

- Fewer Bugs: Two sets of eyes catch errors instantly, often eliminating the need for a separate, time-consuming code review later.

- Knowledge Sharing: It is the fastest way to "onboard" a new team member or teach a junior developer best practices.

- Resilience: If one developer gets sick or leaves, the other knows exactly how the code works, eliminating "bus factors."

- Better Solutions: Collaboration often leads to simpler, more creative solutions than one person could find alone.

## The Challenges

- Exhaustion: Pairing is high-intensity and requires constant communication. Most teams only pair for 4–6 hours a day.

- "Navigator Drift": If the Navigator loses focus, the session becomes one person coding while the other watches.

- Ego: It requires a culture of psychological safety where developers feel comfortable making mistakes in front of each other.
