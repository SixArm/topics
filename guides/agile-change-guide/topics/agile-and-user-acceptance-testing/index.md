# Agile and user acceptance testing (UAT)

In Agile, user acceptance testing (UAT) is a continuous process integrated into each sprint rather than a final phase gate. Teams validate working software against defined acceptance criteria throughout development, catching misunderstandings and defects early when they are cheapest to fix.

Each user story carries specific, measurable acceptance criteria agreed upon before development begins. These criteria serve as the contract between the development team and the product owner, providing an unambiguous standard for what "done" means. When a feature is ready for review, the product owner or designated end-users exercise the software against these criteria, confirming that it meets real business needs rather than just technical specifications.

This approach differs sharply from traditional UAT, where testing happens only after the entire system is built. Agile UAT is sprint-scoped and focused on small increments of functionality, which keeps feedback cycles tight and stakeholder involvement high. If a feature fails acceptance, the feedback flows directly back into the sprint backlog for immediate remediation rather than accumulating in a defect log for later rework.

Product owners play a central role, acting as the voice of the customer and the final authority on acceptance. Their active participation throughout the sprint ensures that the team stays aligned with business priorities and user expectations. Planning UAT sessions in advance, rather than squeezing them into the last day of a sprint, gives adequate time for meaningful evaluation.

Where acceptance criteria are objective and repeatable, teams can automate UAT checks to accelerate regression testing. However, exploratory and subjective validation still requires human judgment, making UAT a blend of automated efficiency and hands-on user verification.
