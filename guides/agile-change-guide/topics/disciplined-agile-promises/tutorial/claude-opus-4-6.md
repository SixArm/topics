# Disciplined Agile promises

In Disciplined Agile (DA), the Promises are commitments that team members make to one another and to the broader organization. While the DA Principles articulate the philosophical "Why" behind disciplined delivery, the Promises define the behavioral "How" — a social contract that translates guiding beliefs into daily action. These seven promises establish expectations for how individuals and teams operate, communicate, and improve. They are not aspirational slogans; they are deliberate commitments that every team member agrees to uphold. When adopted consistently, they create the conditions under which agile delivery thrives: trust, transparency, collaboration, and a relentless focus on delivering value.

## The Seven Promises at a Glance

Disciplined Agile defines exactly seven promises. Each one addresses a specific behavioral dimension that research and practice have shown to be critical for high-performing teams. The table below provides a concise reference.

| # | Promise | Core Commitment |
|---|---------|-----------------|
| 1 | Create Psychological Safety and Trust | Build an environment where people can speak up, experiment, and fail without fear |
| 2 | Collaborate Proactively | Reach across boundaries and share knowledge without waiting to be asked |
| 3 | Make All Work and Workflow Visible | Use visual management to expose the true state of work, bottlenecks, and hidden tasks |
| 4 | Keep Workloads Within Capacity | Limit work in progress to prevent overload, mistakes, and burnout |
| 5 | Accelerate Value Realization | Focus on finishing high-value work quickly rather than staying busy with low-priority items |
| 6 | Be Transparent | Report honestly about project state, including risks, delays, and failures |
| 7 | Improve Continuously | Refuse to accept the status quo; constantly experiment with better ways of working |

## Create Psychological Safety and Trust

Psychological safety is the foundation upon which every other promise rests. When team members fear blame for mistakes or retribution for raising concerns, they stop surfacing problems early, avoid creative experimentation, and default to safe but suboptimal behaviors. The promise to create psychological safety means that the team actively cultivates an environment where candor is rewarded, not punished.

Trust in this context is not a passive sentiment. It is earned through consistent action over time. Leaders and peers demonstrate trustworthiness by following through on commitments, admitting their own mistakes openly, and responding to vulnerability with support rather than judgment. Teams that honor this promise typically exhibit several observable behaviors:

- Retrospective discussions surface genuine problems rather than sanitized summaries
- Junior team members challenge technical decisions made by senior members without hesitation
- Failed experiments are treated as learning opportunities and discussed openly
- Individuals volunteer mistakes early before they compound into larger issues

Without psychological safety, the remaining six promises collapse. Transparency becomes performative, collaboration becomes shallow, and continuous improvement stalls because no one is willing to acknowledge what needs improving.

## Collaborate Proactively

Proactive collaboration means that team members do not wait for formal requests, meetings, or escalation paths to share knowledge and offer help. When someone notices a colleague struggling with a problem, they step in. When a decision in one team will affect another, they reach out early. This promise directly counters the organizational silos and territorial behavior that plague many enterprises.

The word "proactively" is critical. Reactive collaboration — responding when asked — is the minimum standard. Disciplined Agile raises the bar by expecting team members to anticipate needs, seek out cross-functional perspectives, and volunteer information that others might find useful. In practice, this looks like:

- Developers attending design reviews to provide feasibility feedback before designs are finalized
- Operations staff participating in sprint planning to flag deployment risks early
- Team members from adjacent teams sharing lessons learned without being prompted
- Individuals who finish their work early asking where they can contribute rather than waiting for assignment

Proactive collaboration accelerates flow by reducing handoff delays and preventing the information asymmetries that cause rework.

## Make All Work and Workflow Visible

Hidden work is one of the most common sources of dysfunction in technology organizations. Untracked tasks, informal commitments, and invisible dependencies create an illusion of capacity while silently consuming it. The promise to make all work and workflow visible demands that teams use visual management techniques — such as Kanban boards, cumulative flow diagrams, and sprint backlogs — to expose the true state of their work.

Visibility serves multiple purposes:

- **Bottleneck identification**: When work queues are visible, teams can see where items pile up and address constraints systematically rather than through heroic individual effort.
- **Realistic planning**: Visible workflow data enables evidence-based forecasting instead of optimistic guessing.
- **Accountability without micromanagement**: When the board tells the story, managers do not need to ask for status updates.
- **Cross-team coordination**: Shared visibility across teams reduces duplication and exposes dependencies before they cause delays.

The commitment is to make all work visible, not just the work that fits neatly into a tracking tool. This includes interrupts, technical debt remediation, support escalations, and the overhead of meetings and administrative tasks. Teams that hide certain categories of work from their boards systematically undercount their load and overcommit.

## Keep Workloads Within Capacity

This promise directly addresses one of the most damaging patterns in technology work: chronic overcommitment. When teams or individuals take on more work than they can realistically complete, quality degrades, cycle times increase, stress rises, and burnout follows. The promise to keep workloads within capacity is a deliberate commitment to sustainable pace.

The primary mechanism for honoring this promise is limiting work in progress (WIP). WIP limits force teams to finish existing work before starting new work, which has several well-documented effects:

| Without WIP Limits | With WIP Limits |
|---------------------|-----------------|
| Context switching between many items | Focused attention on fewer items |
| Long cycle times due to multitasking overhead | Shorter cycle times through single-piece flow |
| Delayed feedback because items linger in progress | Faster feedback because items finish quickly |
| Hidden bottlenecks masked by busy-ness | Exposed bottlenecks that can be addressed |
| Burnout from sustained overload | Sustainable pace that preserves team health |

Keeping workloads within capacity requires honest assessment of team capacity, which in turn depends on the previous promises of transparency and visibility. Teams cannot manage what they cannot see, and they cannot be honest about capacity if they lack psychological safety.

## Accelerate Value Realization

The promise to accelerate value realization shifts the team's focus from activity to outcome. Being busy is not the same as delivering value. This promise asks teams to prioritize work that reaches customers and stakeholders quickly, rather than optimizing for utilization or pursuing low-priority items that happen to be easy.

Several practices support this promise:

- **Small batch sizes**: Breaking work into the smallest increment that delivers meaningful value, enabling faster delivery and earlier feedback.
- **Prioritization by value**: Using techniques such as weighted shortest job first (WSJF) or cost of delay to ensure the highest-value items are completed first.
- **Reducing cycle time**: Identifying and eliminating delays in the workflow — handoffs, approvals, environment provisioning — that slow delivery without adding value.
- **Minimizing work in progress**: Completing items quickly rather than starting many items simultaneously, which directly connects to the previous promise about capacity.

This promise also implies saying "no" or "not now" to work that does not justify its cost of delay. Teams that try to do everything simultaneously deliver nothing quickly. The discipline lies in making deliberate choices about what to work on and what to defer.

## Be Transparent

Transparency in Disciplined Agile goes beyond posting a status dashboard. It is a commitment to honest, unfiltered communication about the true state of work, including uncomfortable truths about risks, delays, and failures. This promise replaces what many organizations call "watermelon reporting" — projects that appear green on the outside but are red on the inside.

Being transparent requires courage, which is why this promise depends heavily on the first promise of psychological safety. In organizations where bad news is punished, rational actors hide bad news. DA recognizes this dependency and addresses it structurally: the team promises to create an environment where honesty is safe, and then promises to be honest within that environment.

Transparency manifests in specific behaviors:

- Sprint reviews show actual working software rather than slide decks describing planned software
- Risk registers are updated honestly and reviewed regularly rather than treated as checkbox compliance artifacts
- Teams report velocity and cycle time data without adjusting numbers to match expectations
- Impediments are escalated promptly rather than absorbed silently
- Stakeholders receive realistic forecasts based on empirical data rather than optimistic promises

The organizational benefit of transparency is better decision-making. Leaders who receive accurate information about project state can allocate resources effectively, adjust priorities in time, and avoid the costly surprises that come from discovering problems late.

## Improve Continuously

The seventh promise is a commitment to never accepting the current way of working as good enough. Teams that honor this promise actively seek better approaches, run experiments, measure results, and adapt. Continuous improvement is not limited to retrospectives; it is woven into daily work.

Disciplined Agile provides a rich toolkit to support this promise. The DA process decision framework offers teams a catalog of techniques for every aspect of delivery — from requirements management to testing to release — along with guidance on which techniques tend to work better in which contexts. This toolkit turns continuous improvement from an abstract aspiration into a concrete practice: teams can consult the framework, identify alternative approaches, try them, and evaluate the results.

Key aspects of continuous improvement in DA include:

- **Guided experimentation**: Rather than changing everything at once, teams make small, deliberate changes and observe their effects.
- **Context-sensitive adaptation**: What works for one team may not work for another. DA explicitly acknowledges this and encourages teams to tailor their processes.
- **Learning from the broader ecosystem**: Teams look beyond their own experience, studying industry practices, attending communities of practice, and sharing knowledge across the organization.
- **Measuring what matters**: Improvement requires measurement. Teams track metrics such as cycle time, defect rates, and customer satisfaction to determine whether changes are actually producing better outcomes.

## How the Promises Reinforce Each Other

The seven promises are not independent commitments operating in isolation. They form a reinforcing system where each promise creates conditions that enable the others. Understanding these interdependencies helps teams see why partial adoption often fails to produce the expected results.

| Enabling Promise | Enables | Mechanism |
|------------------|---------|-----------|
| Psychological Safety | Transparency | People share bad news only when it is safe to do so |
| Transparency | Continuous Improvement | Teams can only improve what they can see and measure honestly |
| Visibility | Capacity Management | Workloads can only be managed when all work is tracked |
| Capacity Management | Value Acceleration | Teams with sustainable workloads finish items faster |
| Proactive Collaboration | Visibility | Cross-team sharing surfaces hidden work and dependencies |
| Continuous Improvement | All Promises | The improvement cycle strengthens every other commitment |

This interdependence means that teams cannot simply pick their three favorite promises and ignore the rest. A team that commits to transparency but neglects psychological safety will find that transparency quickly erodes. A team that limits work in progress but hides certain categories of work will not see the benefits of WIP limits. The promises work as a complete system.

## Adopting the Promises in Practice

Introducing the DA promises to a team is not a one-time event but a gradual process of building shared understanding and accountability. Teams that successfully adopt the promises typically follow a progression:

- **Start with psychological safety**: Address the foundational promise first. If people do not feel safe, none of the other promises will take hold authentically.
- **Make work visible early**: Visual management provides concrete evidence that the promises are being honored. It also surfaces the issues that the other promises are designed to address.
- **Introduce WIP limits incrementally**: Rather than imposing strict limits immediately, start with soft limits and tighten them as the team builds confidence in the practice.
- **Use retrospectives to assess promise adherence**: Periodically ask the team how well they are honoring each promise and where they see gaps.
- **Extend beyond the team**: As the team matures, extend the promises to interactions with stakeholders, adjacent teams, and leadership.

The promises are most effective when they are treated as a living agreement rather than a static document. Teams should revisit them regularly, discuss whether they are being honored, and identify concrete actions to close gaps.

## Related

Teams exploring the DA promises should also study related topics to build a complete understanding of the Disciplined Agile framework. The DA Principles provide the philosophical foundation that the promises operationalize, so understanding both layers together is essential. The DA Guidelines offer practical recommendations for applying principles and promises in specific delivery contexts. Beyond DA itself, teams benefit from studying psychological safety research, particularly Amy Edmondson's work on fearless organizations. Lean thinking and Kanban practices provide the theoretical basis for WIP limits, flow optimization, and visual management. The Agile Manifesto and its twelve principles remain relevant as the historical foundation upon which DA builds its more comprehensive framework.

## Summary

The Disciplined Agile promises are seven behavioral commitments that transform abstract agile philosophy into a concrete social contract for teams. They address the most critical dimensions of effective teamwork: safety, collaboration, visibility, capacity, value delivery, honesty, and continuous learning. These promises form an interdependent system where each commitment enables and reinforces the others, making partial adoption significantly less effective than full adoption. When honored consistently, they create the conditions under which teams deliver value sustainably, communicate honestly, and improve relentlessly — moving beyond agile as a methodology toward agile as a daily discipline.
