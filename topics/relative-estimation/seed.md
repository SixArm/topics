# Relative Estimation

Relative Estimation is a technique for assessing a value by comparing items to one another, rather than using absolute units such as metres, seconds, grams. 

In the context of startup analytics and validation, this method is highly effective for task estimation, because humans are naturally better at comparing items than predicting exact durations, especially when dealing with the high uncertainty of early-stage products.  

## How it Works

Instead of asking "How long will this take?", your team asks, "Is this task more or less difficult than the one we just finished?".  

* Establish a Baseline: Identify a "standard" task that everyone understands (e.g., creating a simple login page) and assign it a middle value, like a "3" on a Fibonacci scale.
* The "Anchor" System: Every new task is then "anchored" to this baseline. Is a new analytics dashboard twice as hard? Then it’s an 8.
* Unitless Measures: Common scales include Fibonacci numbers (1, 2, 3, 5, 8...), T-Shirt sizes (S, M, L, XL), or even "Coffee Cup" sizes.  

## Why Startups Use It for Validation

* Speeds Up Planning: Precise time estimates are often wrong in startups; relative sizing is faster and allows the team to focus on delivery rather than "fake" precision.  
* Uncovers Hidden Complexity: Because it requires comparing tasks, it forces team discussions that surface unknown risks before they derail a sprint.  
* Focuses on "Effort" not "Hours": It accounts for differences in team member experience. A senior dev might finish a "3-point" task in two hours, while a junior takes a day, but the complexity of the task remains the same.  
* Predicts Velocity: Over 2–3 sprints, you’ll see how many "points" your team typically completes. This "Velocity" allows you to forecast your product roadmap more accurately than guessing dates.  

## Standard Techniques

* Planning Poker: A gamified consensus approach where team members reveal their "point" estimates simultaneously to avoid bias.
* Silent Grouping (Affinity Estimating): The team places task cards on a physical or digital wall, moving them left (easier) or right (harder) without talking, then grouping them into sizes.
