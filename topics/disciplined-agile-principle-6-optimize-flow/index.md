# Disciplined Agile principle 6: Optimize flow

Disciplined Agile's sixth principle states: optimize flow by streamlining the end-to-end delivery of value across the entire organization, not just within a single team. This principle shifts attention from local team efficiency to the smooth movement of work from concept to customer.

Optimizing flow means examining the whole system. A development team might be highly productive, but if work stalls for weeks in testing, legal review, or deployment queues, that speed is wasted. The goal is to identify and eliminate bottlenecks across the entire value stream so that value reaches customers without unnecessary delays.

Visualization is a core practice for achieving flow. Techniques like Kanban boards make work visible, revealing where items pile up and where capacity is misaligned. When teams can see congestion forming, they can address it immediately rather than discovering problems after deadlines slip.

Reducing waste is equally important. This means removing activities that do not contribute to customer value, such as excessive documentation, redundant handoffs, long approval wait times, and gold-plating features that nobody requested. Every step in the process should earn its place by contributing directly to the outcome.

Limiting work in progress is one of the most effective flow strategies. Teams that start many items simultaneously finish fewer of them. By constraining how much work is active at any time, teams complete items faster, reduce context switching, and deliver more predictably.

The deeper insight behind this principle is the distinction between being busy and delivering results. High utilization rates and packed backlogs can mask systemic inefficiency. Optimizing flow prioritizes throughput and cycle time over activity, ensuring that organizations measure success by value delivered rather than effort expended.
