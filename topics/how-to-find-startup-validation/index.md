# How to find startup validation

Startup validation is the process of proving that a business concept addresses a real, urgent problem for a market willing to pay for it. The goal is to replace personal assumptions with empirical data, avoiding the trap of building something nobody wants.

Start by identifying a "Tier 1" problem. A validated problem ranks among the top three most pressing issues your target audience faces. If your idea lands at number five or six on their priority list, they will rarely find the time or budget for it. Look for problems that are urgent, important, and backed by a market with purchasing power. Use tools like Google Trends to confirm growing interest, and mine forums like Reddit to find users demanding a solution in their own words.

Next, conduct non-leading customer interviews. Speak with 30 to 50 potential customers, but avoid pitching your idea upfront. Instead, ask about their world, their pain points, and their past actions. Follow the "Mom Test" approach: ask "When did this problem last happen?" and "What did you do about it?" rather than soliciting opinions about a hypothetical product. Genuine validation sounds like "I need this solved right now," not "That sounds like a nice idea."

Then run smoke tests and pre-sales. Create a simple landing page with a waitlist button and drive targeted traffic to it. A signup conversion rate around 5% is a strong signal. The gold standard is asking for actual payment or, in B2B contexts, a signed letter of intent at a specific price.

Finally, build the smallest possible MVP. Before writing code, deliver the service manually through a concierge MVP. After launching, apply the Sean Ellis benchmark: if 40% or more of users say they would be "very disappointed" without your product, you have achieved initial product-market fit.
