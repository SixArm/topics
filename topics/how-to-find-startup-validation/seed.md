# How to find startup validation

Startup validation is the process of proving that a business concept addresses a real, urgent problem for a market willing to pay for it. The goal is to replace personal assumptions with empirical data to avoid "building something nobody wants".  

## 1. Identify a "Tier 1" Problem

Before testing solutions, you must confirm you are solving an urgent problem.  

* Target the Top 3: A "Tier 1" problem is one of the top three most pressing issues your target audience faces. If your idea is #5 or #6 on their list, they will rarely find the time or budget for it.  
* Criteria for Success: Ideally, your problem should be Urgent, Important, and for a market that has a Budget to solve it.  
* Search and Social Signals: Use [Google Trends](https://trends.google.com/trends/) to see if interest in the problem is growing. Mine [Reddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/SaaS/comments/1nzgoa1/how_do_you_really_validate_your_startup_idea/) or niche forums to find users "shouting" for a solution in their own unfiltered language.  

## 2. Conduct "Non-Leading" Customer Interviews  

The most common validation failure is asking friends for their opinion—they are often too nice to tell you your idea is bad.  

* The 30-Interview Rule: Aim to speak with 30 to 50 potential customers.
* Avoid the Pitch: Do not start by explaining your idea. Instead, ask about their world and existing pain points.
* The "Mom Test" Approach: Ask about their past actions, not future intentions. For example: "When was the last time this problem happened?" and "What did you do to try to fix it?".
* Look for Strong Emotion: Positive validation sounds like "I need this solved right now," not "That's a nice idea".  

## 3. Run "Smoke Tests" and Pre-Sales  

Commitment (time or money) is the only true validation.  

* Landing Page Smoke Test: Create a simple landing page describing your "product" and a "Join Waitlist" or "Get Early Access" button. Drive small amounts of targeted traffic via social media ads to measure the conversion rate—a 5% signup rate is typically a strong signal.  
* Ask for the Sale: The "Gold Standard" of validation is pre-selling. Tell potential users: "I'm building a tool that does X. If I had it today, would you give me your credit card right now?".  
* Letters of Intent (B2B): For business-to-business ideas, seek a signed "Letter of Intent" stating they intend to buy the product at a specific price once it launches.  

## 4. Build the Smallest Possible MVP  

Once you have commitment, build an MVP that focuses exclusively on the "core" value.  

* Do it Manually (Concierge MVP): Before writing code, perform the service manually for a few customers. If you can't deliver value by hand, software won't save the idea.  
* The Sean Ellis Benchmark: After launching a basic version, ask users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this?" If 40% or more say "Very Disappointed," you have achieved initial product-market fit.
