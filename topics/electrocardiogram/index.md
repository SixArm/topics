# electrocardiogram

An electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG) is a diagnostic test that records the heart's electrical signals through electrodes placed on the skin. The output is a time-series waveform graph showing voltage changes per cardiac cycle. It measures heart rate, rhythm regularity, and conduction pathway integrity, making it a foundational tool for detecting arrhythmias, ischemia, and structural damage.

The standard 12-lead ECG uses 10 electrodes on the chest, arms, and legs to capture electrical activity from 12 different vectors. Each lead provides a distinct perspective on the heart's depolarization and repolarization cycle. The key waveform components are the P-wave (atrial depolarization), QRS complex (ventricular depolarization), and the ST segment and T-wave (ventricular repolarization). Deviations in amplitude, duration, or morphology of these components signal specific conditions such as atrial fibrillation, heart block, or myocardial infarction.

Beyond the standard resting ECG, variants include the exercise stress test, which monitors cardiac response under load, and the Holter monitor, a portable device that records continuously over 24 hours or more to catch intermittent events. These are particularly useful for diagnosing conditions that manifest only under specific circumstances.

From a technology perspective, ECG data is a well-structured, high-frequency signal that lends itself to digital processing and algorithmic analysis. Modern systems digitize the waveform for automated interval measurement, anomaly detection, and pattern classification. Machine learning models trained on large ECG datasets can flag abnormalities with high sensitivity, enabling faster triage in clinical workflows. Wearable devices have also brought single-lead ECG capability to consumer hardware, creating new data streams for continuous cardiac monitoring outside clinical settings.

The procedure is noninvasive, safe, and takes only a few minutes for a resting test.
