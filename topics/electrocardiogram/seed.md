# electrocardiogram

An electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG) is a quick, safe, and painless test that records the electrical signals of the heart, used to measure rate, rhythm, and detect potential damage or abnormalities. Electrodes are placed on the skin, and the results are printed as a graph. It is a vital diagnostic tool for cardiac health. 

## What the ECG Measures

- Heart Rate: Measures beats per minute, identifying tachycardia (fast) or bradycardia (slow).
- Rhythm: Assesses the regularity of beats, identifying arrhythmias such as atrial fibrillation.
- Electrical Signals: Monitors the pathway of electrical activity, revealing issues with conduction, such as heart blocks.
- Structural Damage: Detects evidence of current or past heart attacks, as well as muscle hypertrophy. 

## Types of ECG Tests

- Resting ECG: Done while lying down and resting.
- Exercise ECG (Stress Test): Performed while walking on a treadmill or cycling to assess heart function during activity.
- Portable/Holter ECG: A wearable recorder that monitors heart activity over 24 hours or longer, crucial for catching intermittent issues. 

## The Procedure

- Preparation: You may be asked to remove clothing from the top half of your body, and hair may be shaved to ensure electrodes stick properly.
- Application: A technician attaches 10 small, sticky electrodes to your chest, arms, and legs, which are connected by wires to the ECG machine.
- Recording: The machine records your heart's activity for a few minutes while you lie still.
- Risks: It is very safe, with no electricity entering the body. The main side effects are minor skin irritation from the electrodes, similar to removing a plaster. 

## Key Findings

- P-wave: Represents atrial depolarization.
- QRS Complex: Represents ventricular depolarization.
- ST Segment & T-wave: Represent ventricular repolarization.
- Abnormalities: Elevation or depression of these components can indicate ischemia or infarction.
