# The Ringelmann Effect

The Ringelmann Effect states that individual productivity decreases as group size increases. Named after Max Ringelmann, a French agricultural engineer who observed the phenomenon around 1913, it was originally demonstrated through rope-pulling experiments: when people pulled together, each individual exerted less force than when pulling alone. The concept was later termed "social loafing" in psychology and has been validated across cognitive tasks and workplace settings.

In software teams, this effect manifests in two primary ways. First, social loafing occurs when individuals reduce their effort because their contributions are less visible in a larger group, or because they assume others will compensate. Second, coordination overhead grows as team size increases. More people means more communication, more meetings, and more time spent on alignment rather than productive work. Merging code, resolving conflicts, and reaching design consensus all consume time that scales non-linearly with headcount.

The practical implication is that adding team members can make each existing member less efficient. Along with Brooks's Law, the Ringelmann Effect demonstrates that team output does not scale linearly with team size. There is a point at which adding people yields diminishing returns, or even negative returns per person.

Effective countermeasures include keeping teams small and focused, assigning clear ownership and responsibilities, and structuring work to minimize coordination dependencies. This thinking motivates practices like Amazon's "Two Pizza Rule," which caps team size at roughly five to eight people. In brainstorming sessions, breakout groups consistently outperform large-group discussions where many participants stay silent.

Small, focused teams with strong individual accountability consistently outperform poorly coordinated larger teams, making deliberate team sizing one of the highest-leverage organizational decisions in technology.
