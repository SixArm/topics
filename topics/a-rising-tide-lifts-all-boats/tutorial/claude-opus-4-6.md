# A rising tide lifts all boats

"A rising tide lifts all boats" is a metaphorical expression conveying the idea that when an overall environment or economic condition improves, it benefits everyone involved, regardless of their individual circumstances or positions. Often attributed to John F. Kennedy, who used it in a 1963 speech to advocate for broad-based economic growth, the phrase has become a foundational concept in business strategy, technology leadership, and organizational culture. For technology professionals, understanding this principle is essential for building platforms, ecosystems, and teams that generate shared value rather than zero-sum competition.

## Origin and Context

The phrase gained prominence when President Kennedy used it to argue that macroeconomic growth would benefit all Americans, from the wealthiest to the most disadvantaged. The underlying logic is straightforward: when the overall environment improves, participants within that environment gain access to better conditions, more resources, and greater opportunity.

In the decades since, the metaphor has been adopted across industries. In technology, it is used to describe platform economics, open-source ecosystems, industry standards, and collaborative innovation. The core insight is that expanding the total value available in a system can be more impactful than competing over a fixed pool of resources.

## Application in Technology

Technology professionals encounter the rising-tide dynamic in several contexts:

- **Platform ecosystems**: When a platform like AWS, Android, or Shopify grows its user base and capabilities, third-party developers, integrators, and service providers all benefit from the expanded market.
- **Open-source communities**: Contributing to shared codebases raises quality and capability for every user and contributor, reducing duplicated effort across the industry.
- **Industry standards**: Adopting common protocols (HTTP, OAuth, OpenAPI) lowers integration costs for all participants, enabling interoperability and faster innovation.
- **Developer experience investments**: When an organization improves its internal tooling, CI/CD pipelines, or documentation, every engineering team that uses those shared resources becomes more productive.
- **Market expansion**: When a new technology category gains mainstream adoption (e.g., cloud computing, mobile devices, AI), it creates demand that benefits incumbents and newcomers alike.

## Business Strategy Implications

Leaders and strategists use this principle to evaluate whether their actions expand or merely redistribute value. The distinction matters for long-term positioning.

| Strategy Type | Rising-Tide Approach | Zero-Sum Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Market focus | Grow the total addressable market | Capture share from competitors |
| Partnerships | Build ecosystems with shared incentives | Lock in exclusive agreements |
| Innovation | Contribute to open standards and shared tooling | Hoard proprietary advantages |
| Talent | Invest in industry-wide training and community | Recruit aggressively from competitors |
| Pricing | Create value that justifies premium pricing | Undercut competitors on price |

Organizations that consistently pursue rising-tide strategies tend to build stronger ecosystems, attract more partners, and benefit from network effects that compound over time.

## Organizational and Team Dynamics

Within organizations, the metaphor applies to how teams and individuals relate to shared success:

- **Knowledge sharing**: Teams that document decisions, share post-mortems, and maintain internal wikis raise the competence of the entire organization.
- **Infrastructure investment**: Shared platforms, common libraries, and centralized observability tools benefit every team that builds on them.
- **Mentorship and coaching**: Senior engineers who invest in growing junior colleagues strengthen the overall capability of the engineering organization.
- **Psychological safety**: When team culture supports experimentation and learning from failure, innovation increases across the board.

The opposite dynamic is equally instructive. When teams hoard information, duplicate effort, or optimize only for their own metrics at the expense of others, the organization suffers from fragmentation and misalignment.

## Limitations and Criticisms

The metaphor is powerful but not without important caveats:

- **Unequal distribution**: A rising tide may lift all boats, but not equally. Larger, better-positioned organizations or individuals often capture a disproportionate share of the gains. In technology, platform owners typically benefit more than third-party developers.
- **Structural barriers**: Some participants lack the resources, access, or positioning to benefit from a general improvement. A startup without capital cannot take advantage of a booming market the same way an established firm can.
- **Complacency risk**: The phrase can be misused to justify inaction on inequality or to avoid addressing systemic problems, under the assumption that growth alone will solve distributional issues.
- **Dependency and lock-in**: Ecosystem participants who rely heavily on a platform's rising tide become vulnerable if the platform changes direction, adjusts pricing, or deprioritizes their segment.

Technology professionals should use the principle as a strategic lens, not as an excuse to ignore the specific conditions and power dynamics that shape who actually benefits.

## Practical Guidelines for Technology Leaders

To apply the rising-tide principle effectively:

1. **Invest in shared infrastructure** before building bespoke solutions for individual teams.
2. **Contribute to open standards and open-source projects** that expand the overall market or capability set.
3. **Measure ecosystem health**, not just your own metrics. Track partner satisfaction, developer adoption, and community growth.
4. **Design incentive structures** that reward collaboration and shared outcomes, not just individual or team-level performance.
5. **Acknowledge unequal impact** and actively work to ensure that smaller participants, junior team members, and underrepresented groups can access the benefits of collective growth.

## Related

Related topics to explore include network effects and how they amplify rising-tide dynamics, platform strategy and ecosystem design, open-source governance and community building, collaborative advantage as a complement to competitive advantage, servant leadership and its emphasis on elevating the team, the tragedy of the commons as the inverse failure mode, and total addressable market analysis for understanding how market expansion benefits participants.

## Summary

"A rising tide lifts all boats" is a principle that encourages technology professionals to think beyond zero-sum competition and invest in expanding shared value. Whether applied to platform ecosystems, open-source communities, organizational culture, or market strategy, the core insight is that improving the overall environment tends to benefit all participants. However, the principle must be applied with awareness of its limitations: benefits are rarely distributed equally, structural barriers can prevent some from participating, and the metaphor should not be used to justify complacency about inequality. The most effective technology leaders use this principle as a call to build systems, cultures, and strategies that generate broadly shared growth while actively addressing the gaps that prevent universal benefit.
