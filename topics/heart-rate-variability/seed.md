# Heart Rate Variability

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) is the fluctuation in time between consecutive heartbeats, serving as a key indicator of autonomic nervous system health, stress recovery, and physical fitness. Unlike heart rate, a higher HRV is generally better, indicating a flexible system adapting to stress and recovery. It is measured in milliseconds, commonly by wearables, with lower HRV often signaling fatigue or stress.

Key Aspects of HRV

- Physiological Basis: Your heart does not beat like a metronome; it skips beats slightly based on breathing, stress, and nervous system activity.
- Autonomic Balance: HRV measures the balance between the sympathetic (fight or flight) and parasympathetic (rest and digest) branches of your nervous system.
- Key Indicators: High HRV means your body is resilient and recovered. Low HRV may indicate high stress, illness, or fatigue.

Factors Influencing HRV

- Age: HRV typically decreases as you get older.
- Lifestyle: Sleep quality, alcohol consumption, diet, and regular exercise significantly impact HRV.
- Stress: Chronic stress keeps the sympathetic system active, leading to lower HRV.
- Hormones: Menstrual cycles can cause fluctuations, notes.

How to Improve HRV

- Prioritize Sleep: Consistent, high-quality sleep is critical.
- Manage Stress: Utilize meditation, breathing exercises, or yoga to increase parasympathetic activity.
- Exercise Smart: Regular physical activity increases HRV, but overtraining can decrease it.
- Hydration & Diet: Proper nutrition and limiting alcohol improve recovery.

Measuring HRV

- While professional monitoring (EKG) is the most accurate, many consumer devices (like Garmin, WHOOP, and Oura Ring) measure HRV, often during sleep to provide a consistent reading.
