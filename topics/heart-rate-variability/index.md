# Heart Rate Variability

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) is the variation in time intervals between consecutive heartbeats, measured in milliseconds. Rather than beating like a clock, the heart continuously adjusts its rhythm in response to breathing, stress, physical demand, and autonomic nervous system activity. HRV quantifies these fluctuations and serves as a proxy for how well the body adapts to changing conditions.

HRV reflects the balance between the sympathetic nervous system, which drives the fight-or-flight response, and the parasympathetic nervous system, which governs rest and recovery. A higher HRV generally indicates a more resilient and adaptable cardiovascular system. A lower HRV often correlates with chronic stress, fatigue, illness, or overtraining. This makes HRV a practical biomarker for recovery readiness and overall autonomic health.

Several factors influence HRV. Age is the strongest predictor, with HRV declining over time. Sleep quality, exercise habits, hydration, diet, alcohol intake, and chronic stress levels all play significant roles. Hormonal cycles can also cause measurable fluctuations.

From a measurement standpoint, clinical electrocardiograms (ECGs) provide the highest accuracy. However, consumer wearables from companies such as Garmin, WHOOP, Apple, and Oura now estimate HRV using optical sensors, typically capturing readings during sleep for consistency. Common metrics include RMSSD (root mean square of successive differences) and SDNN (standard deviation of normal-to-normal intervals), both derived from inter-beat interval data.

For technology professionals, HRV is relevant in wearable device engineering, health data platforms, and wellness application design. It is a core data stream in digital health APIs and increasingly used in workplace wellness programs, sports analytics, and clinical remote monitoring systems. Understanding HRV enables better decisions when building or evaluating products that depend on physiological signal processing.
