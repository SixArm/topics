# Minto Pyramid Principle

The Minto Pyramid Principle is a communication framework designed to structure complex information into a clear, persuasive, and efficient hierarchy. Developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey & Company, it is the global standard for executive communication and consulting.

## 1. The Core Structure: Top-Down Communication

The framework flips traditional storytelling on its head by delivering the "punchline" first.

- Level 1: The Answer (The Tip) – Start with your main recommendation, conclusion, or governing thought. This is often called BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front).
- Level 2: Supporting Arguments – Group your logic into a few (ideally three) key arguments that prove your answer.
- Level 3: Supporting Data (The Base) – Provide the granular facts, figures, and analysis that back up each middle-level argument.

## 2. The SCQA Framework: Setting the Stage

Minto suggests using the SCQA method to introduce your communication, ensuring the audience agrees on the context before you present your solution:

- Situation: Describe the current, non-controversial status quo.
- Complication: Identify a change or problem that creates an issue.
- Question: State the question that naturally arises from the complication (e.g., "How do we fix this?").
- Answer: Present your main recommendation (the top of your pyramid) as the solution.

## 3. Logical Rules for the Pyramid

For the structure to hold, Minto establishes three critical rules:

1.  Summarisation: Ideas at any level must summarize the ideas grouped below them.
2.  Consistency: Ideas in each group must be of the same "kind" (e.g., all are "reasons" or all are "actions").
3.  Logical Order: Ideas must follow a logical sequence, such as chronological (time), structural (components), or comparative (importance).

## 4. The MECE Principle

A cornerstone of the Minto method is ensuring that your supporting points are MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive):

- Mutually Exclusive: Points should not overlap; each should be a distinct idea.
- Collectively Exhaustive: Together, the points should cover all possible aspects of the problem with no gaps.

## Why It Works

Business leaders are often time-poor and decision-focused. Leading with the answer allows them to understand your conclusion immediately and choose which supporting details they want to dive into, rather than being forced to sit through a "long-winded" journey toward a final reveal.
