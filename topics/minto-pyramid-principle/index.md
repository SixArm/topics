# Minto Pyramid Principle

The Minto Pyramid Principle is a communication framework that structures complex information into a clear, persuasive hierarchy. Developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey & Company, it has become the global standard for executive communication and consulting deliverables.

The framework inverts traditional storytelling by delivering the conclusion first. At the top sits your main recommendation or governing thought, often called BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front). Below that, a few key supporting arguments prove the answer. At the base, granular facts, figures, and analysis back up each argument. This top-down structure lets decision-makers grasp the conclusion immediately and choose which supporting details to explore.

Minto pairs the pyramid with the SCQA method for introductions. You open with the Situation, a non-controversial description of the status quo. Then you introduce the Complication, a change or problem that disrupts it. The Question arises naturally from that tension. Finally, the Answer presents your recommendation as the pyramid's apex.

For the structure to hold, three logical rules apply. Every idea at a given level must summarize the ideas grouped below it. Ideas within each group must be the same kind, whether reasons, actions, or components. And ideas must follow a logical sequence: chronological, structural, or comparative.

A cornerstone of the method is the MECE principle: supporting points must be Mutually Exclusive, with no overlap, and Collectively Exhaustive, covering all aspects with no gaps.

The approach works because business leaders are time-constrained and decision-focused. Leading with the answer respects their attention and lets them control how deep they go into the supporting evidence.
