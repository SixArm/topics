# Situation Complication Question (SCQ)

The Situation Complication Question (SCQ) framework is a narrative method for structuring
complex information into a compelling story. Developed by Barbara Minto at
McKinsey & Company, it is widely used in consulting to define problems and
persuade decision-makers.

## The Pillars of SCQ

The framework functions by establishing a shared starting point before introducing tension and a resolution.

- Situation: A factual, non-controversial description of the status quo. It provides context that the audience already knows and agrees with.
- Example: "We are a mid-sized retailer with a 15% market share in the footwear category".
- Complication: The "what changed" or "what's wrong". This introduces a problem, threat, or opportunity that makes the current situation unsustainable.
- Example: "However, customer acquisition costs have doubled over the last six months, eroding our profit margins".
- Question: The core problem statement that arises logically from the complication. It clarifies exactly what needs to be solved.
- Example: "How can we reduce acquisition costs while maintaining our current market share?".

## Why It Works

The SCQ framework is highly effective because it mimics natural storytelling.

- Grabs Attention: By starting with a familiar situation and following with a complication, you create "tension" that hooks the reader.
- Builds Consensus: Starting with a mutually agreed-upon situation ensures the audience is "nodding along" before you present your recommendation.
- Inverts the Pyramid: While the SCQA is an introduction, it supports the [Minto Pyramid Principle](https://parametricpro.com/blog/scqa-framework), which encourages leading with the most important information first to respect a busy executive's time.
