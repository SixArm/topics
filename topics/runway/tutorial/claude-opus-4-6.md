# Runway

Runway is a core financial metric in the startup and technology world, representing the amount of time a company can continue operating before it exhausts its available cash. Calculated by dividing total cash reserves by the monthly net burn rate, runway gives founders, executives, and investors a concrete timeline for how long the business can sustain itself at its current pace of spending and revenue. Understanding runway is essential for any technology professional involved in company leadership, product strategy, or fundraising, because it directly shapes hiring plans, product roadmaps, and go-to-market decisions.

## Why Runway Matters

Insufficient runway is consistently cited as one of the top reasons startups fail. When cash runs out before a company reaches profitability or secures additional funding, operations halt regardless of how promising the technology or product might be. Runway is not merely an accounting figure; it is a strategic planning tool that influences every major decision a technology organization makes.

For technology professionals in particular, runway determines whether the team can afford to invest in long-term architecture improvements, take on ambitious feature development, or must instead prioritize short-term revenue generation. Engineering leaders who understand their company's runway can make better tradeoff decisions about technical debt, hiring, and infrastructure spending. Product managers who track runway can sequence their roadmaps to deliver revenue-generating features before cash constraints become critical.

## How to Calculate Runway

Runway is calculated in four steps. The formula itself is straightforward, but accuracy depends on honest accounting of both expenses and revenue.

- **Determine cash on hand.** Sum all available cash, bank balances, and liquid assets that can be converted to cash quickly. Exclude illiquid assets such as equipment or intellectual property that cannot be readily sold.

- **Calculate monthly expenses.** Include all recurring costs: salaries and benefits, office rent, cloud infrastructure, software subscriptions, insurance, legal fees, and any other operating overhead.

- **Determine net burn rate.** Subtract monthly revenue from monthly expenses. If the company earns $50,000 per month and spends $120,000 per month, the net burn rate is $70,000. A company that is already profitable has a negative burn rate and theoretically infinite runway.

- **Divide cash on hand by net burn rate.** If the company has $840,000 in cash and a net burn rate of $70,000 per month, the runway is 12 months.

| Component | Example Value |
|---|---|
| Cash on hand | $840,000 |
| Monthly expenses | $120,000 |
| Monthly revenue | $50,000 |
| Net burn rate | $70,000 |
| Runway | 12 months |

## Gross Burn vs. Net Burn

It is important to distinguish between gross burn rate and net burn rate, as confusing the two leads to dangerously optimistic runway estimates.

| Metric | Definition | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Gross burn rate | Total monthly operating expenses before accounting for revenue | Useful for understanding total cost structure and worst-case scenarios |
| Net burn rate | Monthly expenses minus monthly revenue | The standard metric for calculating runway and planning fundraising timelines |

Pre-revenue startups have identical gross and net burn rates. As revenue grows, the gap between the two widens, and net burn becomes the more meaningful figure for planning purposes. However, gross burn remains relevant for stress-testing scenarios where revenue could decline suddenly due to customer churn or market disruption.

## Healthy Runway Targets

A widely accepted benchmark is to maintain 18 to 24 months of runway. This range provides enough time to execute on product milestones, demonstrate traction to investors, and account for the inherent unpredictability of both markets and fundraising cycles.

- **Less than 6 months.** The company is in urgent danger. Fundraising, drastic cost-cutting, or an acqui-hire conversation should already be underway.

- **6 to 12 months.** The company should be actively fundraising or aggressively pursuing profitability. There is limited room for experimentation or pivoting.

- **12 to 18 months.** A reasonable position if the company is executing well, but fundraising preparations should begin soon, particularly if the next round will require significant investor due diligence.

- **18 to 24 months.** The target range for most startups after a funding round. This provides adequate buffer for product development cycles, market testing, and the time required to close a subsequent round.

- **More than 24 months.** A strong position that allows for strategic patience. The company can pursue longer-term bets, weather market downturns, and negotiate future funding from a position of strength.

## When to Start Fundraising

A critical rule of thumb is to begin the fundraising process when 8 to 10 months of runway remain. Fundraising is time-consuming: identifying investors, scheduling meetings, completing due diligence, negotiating terms, and closing the round typically takes 3 to 6 months even for well-positioned companies. Starting earlier provides a buffer against delays, unfavorable market conditions, or the need to adjust strategy based on investor feedback. Waiting too long compresses the timeline, weakens negotiating leverage, and signals desperation to prospective investors.

## Strategies to Extend Runway

When runway is shorter than desired, companies have several levers to pull. The most effective approach usually combines multiple strategies simultaneously.

- **Reduce the burn rate.** Audit discretionary spending such as marketing campaigns, travel, office perks, and underutilized software subscriptions. Negotiate better terms with vendors. Consider whether all open headcount is truly essential.

- **Increase revenue.** Accelerate sales cycles, raise prices where the market supports it, introduce higher-margin product tiers, or pivot toward customer segments with greater willingness to pay.

- **Leverage contractors and freelancers.** Replace full-time hires with contract workers for non-core functions. This reduces fixed costs like benefits, payroll taxes, and severance obligations while maintaining operational capacity.

- **Pursue non-dilutive funding.** Grants from government programs, research institutions, or industry organizations provide capital without equity dilution. Revenue-based financing and venture debt are additional options for companies with predictable cash flows.

- **Renegotiate payment terms.** Extend payment windows with landlords, vendors, and service providers. Even shifting from monthly to quarterly billing cycles can improve short-term cash position.

## Common Mistakes

Several recurring errors cause technology leaders to misjudge their runway or fail to act on it in time.

- **Overly optimistic revenue projections.** Founders often assume revenue growth will continue at its current trajectory or accelerate. Runway calculations should use conservative revenue estimates and include scenarios where revenue declines.

- **Ignoring one-time expenses.** Large purchases, legal settlements, tax obligations, or infrastructure migrations can consume months of runway in a single event. These should be factored into forward-looking burn calculations.

- **Failing to account for fundraising duration.** Many founders underestimate how long it takes to close a round, particularly in challenging markets. Building in a generous fundraising window is essential.

- **Treating runway as static.** Burn rate changes as the company hires, launches products, or enters new markets. Runway should be recalculated monthly and reviewed alongside updated financial forecasts.

## Summary

Runway is the number of months a company can operate before running out of cash, calculated by dividing available cash by the monthly net burn rate. It is one of the most consequential metrics in startup finance because it determines how much time a team has to achieve profitability, reach key milestones, or secure additional funding. A healthy runway target is 18 to 24 months, with fundraising efforts beginning at 8 to 10 months remaining. Extending runway requires a disciplined combination of cost reduction, revenue growth, and creative financing. Technology professionals who understand and actively manage runway position their organizations to survive downturns, capitalize on opportunities, and negotiate from strength.
