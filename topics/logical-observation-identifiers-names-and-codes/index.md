# Logical Observation Identifiers Names and Codes (LOINC)

Logical Observation Identifiers Names and Codes (LOINC) is a universal standard for identifying laboratory tests, clinical observations, and diagnostic reports. It enables interoperability between electronic health records (EHRs), laboratories, and public health authorities. Created by the Regenstrief Institute in 1994, LOINC facilitates data exchange and pools results for clinical care, research, and outcomes management.

LOINC acts as a standardized "question" code, identifying what was observed or measured. It is often paired with SNOMED CT, which serves as the "answer" code identifying the clinical finding or result value. For example, LOINC identifies the test "Glucose [mass/volume] in blood," while SNOMED CT captures the result "High blood sugar." This contrasts with CPT/HCPCS codes, which are used primarily for billing and administrative claims rather than clinical data exchange.

Each LOINC code is a unique, permanent numeric identifier structured around six axes: Analyte (the substance measured), Property (such as mass or concentration), Time aspect (point-in-time or over a duration), Specimen type (blood, urine, etc.), Scale (quantitative, ordinal, narrative), and Method (the measurement technique). This multi-axis structure ensures precise identification across diverse clinical environments.

LOINC covers a broad scope including laboratory tests in chemistry, hematology, and microbiology, as well as clinical measurements like vital signs, ECG readings, and survey instruments. It is used in over 170 countries and is required by US federal systems for health data interoperability, including Meaningful Use requirements.

The Regenstrief LOINC Mapping Assistant (RELMA) helps organizations map local laboratory test codes to standardized LOINC codes. The standard is freely available, lowering barriers to adoption across healthcare systems worldwide.
