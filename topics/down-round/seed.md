# Down round

A down round is a venture capital funding round where a company issues new shares at a lower valuation—and thus a lower price per share—than in its previous financing round. While typically seen as a negative signal, it is often a necessary "reset" for companies that were previously overvalued or are facing difficult market conditions. 

Why Down Rounds Happen
Missed Milestones: Failure to meet growth, revenue, or product development targets set in earlier stages.
Macroeconomic Shifts: Broad economic downturns, rising interest rates, or sector-specific declines (e.g., tech or crypto).
Increased Competition: New rivals entering the space, reducing the company’s market share or unique competitive edge.
Cash Crunch: Urgent need for capital to maintain operations (runway) when other funding options are exhausted. 

Major Impacts
Severe Dilution: To raise the same amount of capital at a lower price, the company must issue more shares, significantly reducing the ownership percentage of existing shareholders. 

Anti-Dilution Triggers: Preferred investors often have protections (like "full-ratchet" or "weighted average") that grant them extra shares to maintain their value, further diluting founders and employees. 

Employee Morale: Stock options may become "underwater" (the strike price is higher than the current fair market value), making them effectively worthless and hindering retention. 

Negative Signalling: It can damage market confidence, making future fundraising or hiring more challenging. 

Strategic Alternatives
Bridge Financing: Raising short-term capital through Convertible Notes or SAFEs to delay a full priced round.
Reducing Burn: Implementing cost-cutting measures or layoffs to extend existing runway.
Renegotiating Terms: Working with current investors to waive anti-dilution rights or adjust terms in exchange for new capital.
