# Agile crawl, walk, run

The crawl, walk, run approach is a maturity model for implementing new Agile processes, tools, or mindsets without overwhelming the team. It recognizes that Agile excellence cannot be achieved overnight. Teams must master the basics before tackling complex scaling, just as Agile itself delivers software in increments. This phased strategy reduces transformation risk by introducing changes at a pace the organization can absorb, building confidence and capability at each stage before advancing to the next.


## Why phased adoption matters

Introducing too many changes at once often leads to burnout, confusion, and a retreat to old habits. A team that has not yet mastered sprint cadence will struggle if forced into full DevOps automation or cross-team dependency management. The crawl, walk, run model builds psychological safety by allowing teams to fail small during early phases, making it safer to experiment as they advance. Each stage delivers measurable process improvements, creating momentum and confidence that sustains the transformation over time.

Organizations that skip phases tend to experience:

- Surface-level adoption without internalized values
- Tooling that outpaces team understanding
- Metrics that are tracked but not acted upon
- Reversion to waterfall behaviors under delivery pressure


## The three phases

### Crawl phase: Foundation building

In the crawl phase, the team focuses on customer discovery, transparency, and making work visible. This is the foundation where teams learn to collaborate openly, establish basic rituals, and begin thinking in terms of iterative delivery rather than big-bang releases.

Key activities in the crawl phase include:

- Establishing daily standups and regular retrospectives
- Creating a visible backlog using a physical or digital board
- Defining initial user stories with acceptance criteria
- Building trust between team members and stakeholders
- Delivering small increments to gather early feedback

The primary goal is not speed or efficiency but learning. Teams should expect awkwardness and imperfection. Success is measured by whether the team is consistently collaborating, communicating openly, and delivering working software at a predictable cadence, however slow.

### Walk phase: Standardization and rigor

In the walk phase, the team standardizes workflows. This includes establishing a Definition of Done, improving estimation accuracy, and beginning to automate testing. Teams at this stage have internalized Agile fundamentals and are ready to add rigor and repeatability to their practices.

Key activities in the walk phase include:

- Formalizing a Definition of Done and enforcing it consistently
- Introducing velocity tracking and using it for capacity planning
- Automating unit tests and beginning integration testing
- Refining backlog grooming to produce higher-quality stories
- Establishing cross-functional collaboration patterns

### Run phase: Optimization and scaling

In the run phase, the team pursues optimization. This means implementing continuous delivery with DevOps integration, tracking advanced metrics like lead time and cycle time, and scaling Agile across multiple departments or product lines.

Key activities in the run phase include:

- Implementing continuous integration and continuous delivery pipelines
- Tracking flow metrics such as lead time, cycle time, and throughput
- Scaling practices across teams using frameworks like SAFe, LeSS, or Nexus
- Conducting regular value stream mapping to eliminate waste
- Driving organizational-level change beyond the development team


## Phase comparison

| Dimension | Crawl | Walk | Run |
|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Focus | Visibility and learning | Standardization and consistency | Optimization and scaling |
| Delivery cadence | Irregular, improving | Predictable sprints | Continuous flow |
| Testing approach | Manual, exploratory | Automated unit tests, some integration | Full CI/CD with automated regression |
| Metrics tracked | Team happiness, story completion | Velocity, sprint burndown | Lead time, cycle time, deployment frequency |
| Stakeholder engagement | Initial trust-building | Regular demos and feedback | Embedded collaboration, shared OKRs |
| Risk tolerance | High tolerance for mistakes | Moderate, with guardrails | Low tolerance, fast recovery |
| Team autonomy | Guided by coach or lead | Self-organizing within boundaries | Fully autonomous, self-improving |


## How to assess readiness to advance

Teams should not advance to the next phase based on calendar time alone. Readiness is determined by demonstrated capability. Consider these indicators:

**Crawl to Walk readiness:**

- The team consistently holds all ceremonies without being prompted
- Work-in-progress is visible and understood by all team members
- Stakeholders trust the team to deliver iteratively
- The team voluntarily identifies and discusses impediments

**Walk to Run readiness:**

- Sprint commitments are met reliably, with velocity stabilizing
- The Definition of Done is enforced without negotiation
- Automated test coverage provides confidence for refactoring
- The team proactively improves its own process


## Common pitfalls

- **Skipping crawl:** Organizations eager for results jump directly to advanced tooling and metrics. Without the cultural foundation, these become theater rather than transformation.
- **Staying in crawl too long:** Some teams become comfortable with low expectations. A coach or leadership nudge is sometimes necessary to move forward.
- **Treating phases as binary:** The transition between phases is gradual. Some practices may be at the walk stage while others remain at crawl. This is normal and expected.
- **Confusing tools with maturity:** Purchasing enterprise Agile tooling does not advance the team's phase. Maturity is about behavior and mindset, not software licenses.
- **Applying uniformly across teams:** Different teams have different starting points. A newly formed team and a team with five years of Agile experience should not be placed at the same phase.


## Applying to different contexts

The crawl, walk, run model applies beyond software development teams. It is equally useful for:

- **DevOps adoption:** Start with basic version control discipline, advance to automated builds, then achieve full infrastructure-as-code with continuous deployment.
- **Data teams:** Begin with visible analytics backlogs, progress to sprint-based delivery of dashboards, then implement real-time data pipelines with automated quality checks.
- **Leadership transformation:** Start with leaders attending demos, move to leaders removing impediments, then achieve leaders who coach rather than direct.
- **Enterprise portfolio management:** Begin with making all projects visible, advance to prioritization frameworks, then implement lean portfolio management with rolling funding cycles.

## Summary

The crawl, walk, run approach provides a pragmatic, low-risk path to Agile transformation by decomposing the journey into three progressive stages: building foundational visibility and trust, establishing standardized repeatable practices, and finally optimizing for speed and scale. Its power lies in matching the pace of change to the team's demonstrated readiness rather than an arbitrary timeline, ensuring that each capability is genuinely internalized before the next layer of complexity is introduced.
