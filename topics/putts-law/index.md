# Putt's Law

Putt's Law states: "Technology is dominated by two types of people — those who understand what they do not manage, and those who manage what they do not understand." It captures a persistent tension in technology organizations between technical expertise and management authority.

The law highlights a real structural problem. The best engineers rarely end up in management, and those who rise to manage often lack deep technical understanding. A software manager from a sales background might push a team to deliver complex features on an aggressive timeline, not grasping the engineering effort involved. Meanwhile, a skilled systems engineer who knows exactly how to optimize infrastructure has no seat at the table when budgets and priorities are decided.

Putt's corollary makes the problem sharper: "Every technical hierarchy, in time, develops a competence inversion." The people at the top end up knowing less about the actual work than the people below them. This inversion drives dysfunction in both directions. Managers set unrealistic deadlines because they underestimate complexity. Engineers build the wrong things because they lack business context. Both sides feel misunderstood.

Startups often avoid this early on because founders both manage and build. But as organizations grow and roles specialize, the gap widens. The most effective remedy is ensuring technical leadership maintains genuine technical competence. CTOs and tech leads who keep one foot in management and one in engineering serve as a critical bridge.

The practical takeaway is straightforward: organizations that invest in technically literate leadership — whether by training managers in engineering fundamentals or promoting engineers who develop business acumen — consistently outperform those that let the competence inversion take hold unchecked.
