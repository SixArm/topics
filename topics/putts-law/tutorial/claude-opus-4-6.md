# Putt's Law

Putt's Law states: "Technology is dominated by two types of people — those who understand what they do not manage, and those who manage what they do not understand." Coined by Archibald Putt (a pseudonym), this observation captures one of the most persistent structural tensions in technology organizations: the gap between technical expertise and management authority. For technology professionals, understanding Putt's Law is essential for navigating organizational dynamics, advocating for sound decision-making, and building teams that bridge the divide between those who build and those who direct.


## The Core Observation

Putt's Law identifies a fundamental split in technology workplaces. Technical practitioners — engineers, architects, data scientists — possess deep knowledge of systems, constraints, and tradeoffs. Managers and executives control budgets, timelines, and strategic direction. The problem is that these two groups rarely overlap. The best engineers seldom pursue management, and those who rise into leadership roles often lack hands-on technical understanding. This creates a recurring pattern where decisions are made by people who do not fully grasp the technical implications, while those who do grasp them have limited influence over organizational direction.


## Putt's Corollary: The Competence Inversion

Putt's corollary sharpens the original law: "Every technical hierarchy, in time, develops a competence inversion." As organizations grow and layers of management accumulate, the people at the top end up knowing less about the actual work than the people below them. This inversion is not a failure of individuals but a structural outcome of how hierarchies evolve. When promotion criteria favor communication skills, political acumen, or tenure over technical depth, the hierarchy naturally drifts toward a state where authority and expertise are inversely correlated.


## How the Dysfunction Manifests

The competence inversion drives dysfunction in both directions. The following table illustrates common failure modes that arise when management and technical teams operate without mutual understanding:

| Failure Mode | Management Side | Technical Side |
|---|---|---|
| Unrealistic deadlines | Underestimates engineering complexity, commits to timelines without consulting teams | Knows the work cannot be done in time but lacks authority to push back effectively |
| Misallocated resources | Funds initiatives based on business trends rather than technical feasibility | Watches critical infrastructure decay while flashy projects receive investment |
| Scope creep | Agrees to new features mid-cycle without understanding downstream impact | Absorbs additional work silently, leading to burnout and technical debt |
| Poor risk assessment | Dismisses technical warnings as pessimism or resistance to change | Raises concerns that are filtered or softened before reaching decision-makers |
| Wrong technology choices | Selects tools and platforms based on vendor marketing or executive relationships | Inherits technology stacks that do not fit the problem, then must work around limitations |


## Why Organizations Fall Into This Trap

Several forces push organizations toward the competence inversion:

- **Promotion pathways reward generalism.** Engineers who stay deeply technical often hit a career ceiling, while those who shift toward management gain access to higher compensation and authority, regardless of their technical depth.
- **Role specialization increases with scale.** Startups often avoid Putt's Law because founders both manage and build. As organizations grow, roles narrow, and the gap between decision-makers and practitioners widens.
- **Management training ignores technical literacy.** MBA programs and leadership development tracks rarely emphasize the need to understand engineering constraints, system architecture, or technical debt.
- **Communication barriers compound over time.** Engineers and managers develop different vocabularies, different mental models, and different definitions of success. Without deliberate effort, these gaps widen with each organizational layer.


## Strategies for Mitigating the Competence Inversion

The competence inversion is not inevitable. Organizations and individuals can take concrete steps to counteract it:

| Strategy | Description | Who Benefits |
|---|---|---|
| Technically literate leadership | CTOs, VPs of Engineering, and tech leads who maintain genuine hands-on skills serve as a bridge between management and engineering | Entire organization |
| Dual career ladders | Creating a staff/principal/distinguished engineer track with equivalent authority and compensation to management removes the incentive to abandon technical work for career advancement | Senior engineers |
| Embedded technical advisors | Placing experienced engineers in advisory roles alongside executive teams ensures that strategic decisions are informed by technical reality | Executive leadership |
| Management training for engineers | Helping engineers develop business acumen, financial literacy, and communication skills enables them to participate more effectively in organizational decisions | Engineers moving into leadership |
| Engineering exposure for managers | Requiring managers to participate in technical reviews, architecture discussions, or even periodic hands-on work keeps them grounded in the reality of what their teams do | Non-technical managers |
| Transparent decision-making | Making the rationale behind priority, budget, and timeline decisions visible to engineering teams reduces the information asymmetry that fuels dysfunction | Cross-functional teams |


## Recognizing Putt's Law in Practice

Technology professionals can watch for specific warning signs that the competence inversion has taken hold in their organization:

- Deadlines are set before technical teams are consulted about feasibility.
- Architecture and technology decisions are made at the executive level without engineering input.
- Engineers are told to "just make it work" when they raise concerns about technical constraints.
- Management measures success primarily through output metrics (features shipped, story points completed) rather than outcome metrics (system reliability, user satisfaction, technical debt reduction).
- The most technically skilled people in the organization have the least influence over strategic direction.
- Post-mortems identify management decisions as root causes but frame the findings as "process improvements" rather than addressing the underlying competence gap.


## Individual Responses to Putt's Law

For individual technology professionals, Putt's Law offers both a diagnostic framework and a call to action:

- **If you are an engineer:** Develop enough business context to articulate technical decisions in terms that managers and executives understand. Learn to frame technical debt, architectural risk, and engineering effort in terms of business impact — cost, revenue, time-to-market, and risk.
- **If you are a manager:** Invest in understanding the technical fundamentals of what your team builds. You do not need to write production code, but you should be able to follow an architecture discussion, understand why a refactor matters, and recognize when a timeline is unrealistic.
- **If you are a leader:** Build organizational structures that prevent the competence inversion from taking hold. Dual career ladders, technically grounded leadership, and transparent decision-making processes are not perks — they are structural safeguards against the dysfunction Putt's Law describes.


## Related

Putt's Law connects to several related concepts worth exploring. The Peter Principle describes how people in hierarchies tend to rise to their level of incompetence, which is a closely related mechanism. Conway's Law explains how organizational structure shapes technical architecture, making the competence inversion doubly dangerous because poor organizational design produces poor systems. Brooks's Law, from "The Mythical Man-Month," addresses how adding people to a late project makes it later — a common consequence of management decisions made without technical understanding. The Dilbert Principle, proposed by Scott Adams, satirizes the same dynamic by suggesting that the least competent people are promoted to management to limit the damage they can do. Finally, the concept of technical debt, coined by Ward Cunningham, provides a vocabulary for one of the most common casualties of the competence inversion: the gradual accumulation of engineering shortcuts that result from management pressure applied without technical awareness.


## Summary

Putt's Law illuminates a structural tension that technology professionals encounter throughout their careers: the persistent gap between technical expertise and management authority. As organizations grow, hierarchies naturally develop a competence inversion where decision-making power concentrates in the hands of those furthest from the technical work. The most effective organizations counteract this by investing in technically literate leadership, creating dual career ladders, and building transparent decision-making processes that keep engineering and management aligned. For individual professionals, the practical takeaway is to bridge the gap from your side — engineers who develop business acumen and managers who maintain technical grounding are the most valuable people in any technology organization.
