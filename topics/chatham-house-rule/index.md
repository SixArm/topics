# Chatham House Rule

The Chatham House Rule is a globally recognized principle established in 1927 to encourage open, confidential discussion. It states that participants in a meeting may freely use information they receive, but must not reveal the identity or affiliation of the speaker or any other participant. This decouples ideas from their sources, enabling frank debate without fear of attribution.

The rule is particularly valuable in technology contexts where professionals need to discuss sensitive topics such as security incidents, architectural failures, vendor evaluations, or organizational challenges. By removing the risk of personal or professional consequences, participants can share candid assessments and lessons learned that would otherwise remain unspoken.

The official term is singular: "Chatham House Rule," not "Rules." It is distinct from "off the record," which lacks a standardized definition. The rule does not make a meeting secret; rather, it permits sharing what was said while protecting who said it. This makes it well-suited for retrospectives, cross-company working groups, industry roundtables, and advisory boards.

In practice, the rule helps prevent groupthink by allowing open criticism of ideas without political consequences. It is widely adopted in corporate, academic, and government settings where trust and psychological safety are prerequisites for productive conversation. When invoking the rule, facilitators should state it explicitly at the start of a session so all participants share the same expectations about attribution and confidentiality.
