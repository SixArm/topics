# Chatham House Rule

The Chatham House Rule is a globally recognized, morally binding principle created in 1927 to encourage open, confidential discussion. It states that participants are free to use information from a meeting, but cannot reveal the identity or affiliation of the speaker(s) or any other participant, fostering free debate.

## Key Principles of the Rule:

Information Sharing: Participants can use the information received for research, reporting, or further action, but must not disclose who shared it.

Confidentiality: The rule protects the identity of speakers, enabling them to voice controversial, technical, or sensitive views without fear of professional or personal consequences.

Scope: It applies to both the speaker and other participants in the meeting.

Purpose: It is primarily designed to facilitate open discussion in debates, forums, and conferences, particularly when dealing with public policy or high-level business issues.

## Misconceptions and Best Practices:

"Rule" vs. "Rules": The official term is the Chatham House Rule (singular).

Not "Off the Record": Unlike "off the record," which can be subjective, the Chatham House Rule is a standard, defined, and often enforced, standard.

Application: It does not mean the entire meeting is secret; it means the information is decoupled from the source.

Context: It is often used to prevent "groupthink" and allow for open criticism of ideas.
