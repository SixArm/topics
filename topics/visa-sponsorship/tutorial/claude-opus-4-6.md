# Visa sponsorship

Visa sponsorship is the process by which an employer takes legal responsibility for a foreign national's immigration status, enabling that individual to obtain a work visa and lawfully perform employment in a host country. For technology professionals, visa sponsorship is often the critical gateway to international career opportunities, particularly in the United States, where the technology sector is the largest single consumer of employer-sponsored work visas. Understanding the mechanics, timelines, costs, and obligations of visa sponsorship is essential for both employers seeking global talent and professionals navigating cross-border employment.

## How visa sponsorship works

The sponsorship process begins when an employer identifies a foreign national they wish to hire and commits to supporting that person's visa application. In the United States, the employer must first demonstrate that no qualified U.S. workers are available to fill the position, a requirement known as the labor market test. The employer files the appropriate petition with U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS), pays applicable fees, and provides supporting documentation such as job descriptions, proof of the candidate's qualifications, and evidence of the prevailing wage for the role. Once approved, the foreign national can apply for the visa at a U.S. consulate abroad or, if already in the country, adjust their status.

Sponsorship is not a one-time event. Employers must maintain ongoing compliance with visa regulations, including paying the sponsored employee at or above the prevailing wage, providing working conditions consistent with those offered to similarly situated U.S. workers, and notifying USCIS of any material changes in employment.

## The H-1B visa in detail

The H-1B visa is the most widely used work visa for technology professionals. It is designed for workers in specialty occupations that require the theoretical and practical application of a body of specialized knowledge, along with at least a bachelor's degree or its equivalent. Software engineers, data scientists, systems architects, and product managers commonly qualify.

The H-1B program operates under an annual numerical cap of 65,000 visas, with an additional 20,000 reserved for applicants holding a U.S. master's degree or higher. Because demand regularly exceeds supply, USCIS conducts a lottery to select petitions for processing. In recent years, the selection rate has been well below 50 percent, making the lottery a significant bottleneck for employers and candidates alike.

Key aspects of the H-1B process include:

- **Labor Condition Application (LCA):** The employer must file an LCA with the Department of Labor, attesting to the wage, working conditions, and absence of a strike or lockout at the worksite.
- **Prevailing wage determination:** The employer must pay the higher of the actual wage paid to similarly employed workers or the prevailing wage for the occupation in the geographic area.
- **Portability:** An H-1B holder can transfer to a new employer as soon as the new employer files a petition, without waiting for approval.
- **Dual intent:** Unlike many nonimmigrant visas, the H-1B allows the holder to pursue permanent residency (a green card) simultaneously.
- **Cap exemptions:** Certain employers, including universities, nonprofit research organizations, and government research organizations, are exempt from the annual cap.

## Employer obligations and costs

Sponsoring a visa carries significant financial and administrative responsibilities. Employers should be prepared for the following costs and commitments:

- **Filing fees:** USCIS filing fees for an H-1B petition can range from several hundred to several thousand dollars, depending on the employer's size and whether premium processing is elected.
- **Attorney fees:** Immigration attorneys typically charge between $2,000 and $10,000 per case, depending on complexity.
- **Prevailing wage compliance:** Employers must pay at least the prevailing wage for the position and geographic area, which may exceed what they would otherwise offer.
- **Recordkeeping:** Employers must maintain a public access file containing the LCA and supporting documents, available for inspection by the Department of Labor.
- **Non-displacement attestation:** Certain H-1B-dependent employers must attest that they have not displaced and will not displace U.S. workers.
- **Good faith termination obligations:** If an H-1B worker is terminated before the end of the visa period, the employer may be responsible for reasonable return transportation costs.

## The path from work visa to permanent residency

Many technology professionals view a work visa as the first step toward permanent residency in the United States. The most common employer-sponsored green card process follows three stages:

- **PERM Labor Certification:** The employer conducts a recruitment process to demonstrate that no qualified U.S. workers are available. This stage typically takes 6 to 18 months.
- **I-140 Immigrant Petition:** The employer files a petition with USCIS to classify the worker under an immigrant visa category, such as EB-2 (advanced degree) or EB-3 (skilled worker). Processing takes several months to over a year.
- **I-485 Adjustment of Status or Consular Processing:** The worker applies for permanent residency. Wait times depend on the applicant's country of birth and the visa bulletin priority date, and can range from immediate availability to decades for nationals of India and China.

The extended green card backlog for Indian and Chinese nationals is one of the most consequential challenges facing technology professionals, with some estimates placing wait times at 50 years or more for certain categories.

Visa sponsorship is not unique to the United States. Technology professionals should understand how other major destinations approach work authorization:

## Challenges and risks for technology professionals

Navigating visa sponsorship involves several risks that candidates and employers should carefully consider:

- **Lottery uncertainty:** For the H-1B, selection is not guaranteed regardless of qualifications. Employers and candidates must have contingency plans.
- **Employer dependency:** A sponsored worker's legal right to remain in the country is tied to their employer. Changing jobs requires a new petition, and involuntary separation can trigger a short grace period to find new sponsorship or depart.
- **Processing delays:** Government processing times are variable and subject to policy changes, requests for evidence, and administrative backlogs.
- **Policy volatility:** Immigration policy changes with administrations and legislative action. Regulations affecting wage levels, eligibility criteria, and visa availability can shift with limited notice.
- **Visa fraud scrutiny:** USCIS and the Department of Labor actively investigate visa fraud. Employers and workers must ensure that the position, wages, and working conditions are genuine and accurately represented.

## Best practices for candidates seeking sponsorship

Technology professionals seeking visa sponsorship should approach the process strategically:

- **Research employer track records:** U.S. employers' H-1B filings are public record. Review an employer's history of sponsorship, approval rates, and offered wages before accepting an offer.
- **Negotiate timing:** Understand the H-1B registration window, typically in March, and plan job searches accordingly. Missing the window means waiting an additional year.
- **Consider alternative visa categories:** If the H-1B lottery is unfavorable, explore L-1, O-1, TN, or E-2 options depending on qualifications and circumstances.
- **Maintain valid status:** Any gap in immigration status can have severe consequences. Track visa expiration dates, travel restrictions, and filing deadlines meticulously.
- **Engage qualified counsel:** Immigration law is complex and high-stakes. Work with an experienced immigration attorney, ideally one specializing in employment-based immigration for the technology sector.

## Summary

Visa sponsorship is the legal mechanism that enables employers to hire foreign nationals by taking responsibility for their immigration status and work authorization. For technology professionals, the H-1B visa remains the dominant pathway into the United States, though it is constrained by annual caps, lottery selection, and extended green card backlogs. Employers bear significant costs and compliance obligations, while candidates face uncertainty, employer dependency, and policy volatility. A well-informed approach, including understanding alternative visa categories, international options, and the full arc from temporary work authorization to permanent residency, is essential for both employers building global teams and professionals pursuing international careers.
