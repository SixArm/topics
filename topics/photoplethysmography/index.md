# Photoplethysmography (PPG)

Photoplethysmography (PPG) is a non-invasive optical technique that detects blood volume changes in microvascular tissue using light. It is widely deployed in pulse oximeters and wearable devices such as smartwatches and fitness bands. PPG works by shining light into the skin and measuring variations in light absorption or reflection caused by the pulsatile flow of blood with each heartbeat.

PPG sensors pair light sources with photodetectors. Wrist-worn devices typically use green LEDs, while finger-clip oximeters use red and infrared wavelengths. During each cardiac cycle, blood volume in the microvascular bed increases, which raises light absorption and lowers reflection. The photodetector captures these fluctuations and translates them into a waveform signal.

The PPG waveform has two components. The AC (pulsatile) component is driven directly by cardiac activity and carries information about heart rate and rhythm. The DC (non-pulsatile) component reflects baseline absorption from tissue, bone, and average blood volume. Separating these components enables extraction of multiple physiological metrics.

Common applications include continuous heart rate monitoring, pulse oximetry for arterial oxygen saturation, blood pressure estimation, arrhythmia detection (including atrial fibrillation), respiratory rate tracking, sleep staging, and stress assessment. These capabilities make PPG a cornerstone of modern remote health monitoring.

The technology has meaningful limitations. Motion artifacts degrade signal quality significantly, making accurate readings difficult during physical activity. Skin pigmentation, skin thickness, tattoos, and ambient light conditions can all reduce measurement accuracy. Signal processing algorithms continue to improve, but these factors remain active areas of engineering effort.

Originally developed in the 1930s, PPG gained clinical prominence with the introduction of pulse oximetry in the 1970s and has since become ubiquitous through consumer wearables and smartphones.
