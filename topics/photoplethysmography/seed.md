# Photoplethysmography (PPG)

Photoplethysmography (PPG) is a non-invasive, low-cost optical technique that detects blood volume changes in microvascular tissue using light. Primarily used in wearable devices (like smartwatches) and pulse oximeters, PPG shines light into the skin and measures variations in light absorption or reflection caused by heartbeats. 

Key Aspects of PPG Technology:
- How it Works: PPG sensors use light sources (typically green LEDs for wrist-worn devices, or red/infrared for finger oximeters) and photodetectors to measure small changes in blood volume during each cardiac cycle. When the heart beats, blood volume in the microvascular bed increases, resulting in higher light absorption and lower reflection.

Common Applications:
- Heart Rate Monitoring: Used in wearable devices (e.g., Fitbit) to measure heart rate.
- Pulse Oximetry: Measuring arterial oxygen saturation.
- Cardiovascular Monitoring: Assessing blood pressure, cardiac output, and detecting arrhythmias like atrial fibrillation.
- Other Metrics: Used to monitor breathing rate, sleep stages, and stress levels.

Limitations:
- Motion Artifacts: Signal quality is highly affected by movement, which can cause inaccuracies in data.
- External Factors: Skin tone, skin thickness, tattoos, and ambient light can affect accuracy. 

The PPG waveform signal consists of two parts: 
- AC Component (Pulsatile): Driven by cardiac activity.
- DC Component (Non-pulsatile): Attributed to tissue, bone, and average blood volume. 

Evolution:
- Originally developed in the 1930s, PPG gained prominence with the introduction of pulse oximetry in the 1970s and has since revolutionized remote health monitoring through consumer wearables and smartphones.
