# International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems (ICD)

The International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems (ICD) is the global standard for identifying and reporting health conditions. Maintained by the World Health Organization (WHO), it provides a unified system of alphanumeric codes that allow different countries to record, analyze, and compare health data consistently. For technology professionals, ICD is a foundational data standard encountered across healthcare IT, insurance platforms, clinical systems, and public health analytics.

The current version, ICD-11, officially came into effect on January 1, 2022. It was designed for digital-first use, featuring roughly 17,000 unique codes and 120,000 codable terms. ICD-10 remains widely deployed, particularly for morbidity and mortality reporting. Several countries maintain national modifications for clinical billing and reporting, such as ICD-10-CM in the United States and ICD-10-CA in Canada. Systems integrators and developers frequently need to support multiple ICD versions simultaneously during transition periods.

ICD serves as the bedrock for health statistics worldwide. It standardizes the monitoring of disease trends, reporting of causes of death, health resource allocation, hospital administration, and health insurance billing and reimbursement. Any system handling clinical data, claims processing, or epidemiological analysis will interact with ICD codes at some level.

The WHO provides online tools including the ICD-11 Browser for navigating the classification hierarchy, a Coding Tool for mapping clinical diagnoses to codes, and reference guides covering coding rules and implementation. For developers building healthcare applications, ICD compliance is typically a regulatory requirement, and the structured, hierarchical nature of the coding system lends itself well to database modeling, search indexing, and interoperability with other standards such as SNOMED CT and LOINC.
