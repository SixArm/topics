# International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems (ICD)

The International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems (ICD) is the global standard for identifying and reporting health conditions. 

Maintained by the World Health Organization (WHO), it provides a unified system of alphanumeric codes that allow different countries to record, analyze, and compare health data consistently.

## Current Status and Versions

ICD-11: The latest version officially came into effect on 1 January 2022. It is designed for use in a digital world, featuring roughly 17,000 unique codes and 120,000 codable terms.

ICD-10: While many countries are transitioning to ICD-11, the ICD-10 version remains widely used for reporting morbidity (illness) and mortality (death) data.

National Modifications: Some countries use adapted versions for clinical billing and reporting, such as the ICD-10-CM in the United States or ICD-10-CA in Canada.

## Primary Uses

The ICD is the "bedrock" for health statistics and is used for:

- Monitoring Health Trends: Tracking the spread of diseases and identifying global health threats.

- Causes of Death: Standardising how causes of death are reported on certificates worldwide.

- Health Management: Assisting in resource allocation, hospital administration, and health policy planning.

- Financial Administration: Serving as the foundation for health insurance billing and reimbursement systems in many countries.

## Access and Tools

The WHO provides several online resources to help professionals use the system:

- ICD-11 Browser: A tool for navigating the classification and seeing specific entities.
 
- Coding Tool: Helps users find the correct code for a specific clinical diagnosis.

- Reference Guides: Detailed manuals like the ICD-11 Reference Guide provide instructions on coding rules and implementation.
