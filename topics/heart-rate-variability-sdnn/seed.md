# Heart rate variability SDNN

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) SDNN (Standard Deviation of Normal-to-Normal
intervals) is a metric measuring the standard deviation of time intervals
between heartbeats, typically in milliseconds (ms). It represents overall
autonomic nervous system activity (both sympathetic and parasympathetic) and is
considered a "gold standard" for cardiac risk, with higher values (typically 100
ms) indicating better cardiovascular health and lower stress.Key Aspects of
SDNNDefinition: SDNN measures how much your HRV varies from the average over a
specific time, commonly 24 hours (clinical setting) or shorter periods.Clinical
Significance: Often used to measure cardiac health; studies show that patients
with SDNN values below 50 ms are classified as having compromised health, while
those over 100 ms are considered healthy.Higher vs. Lower: Higher SDNN signifies
a well-adapted autonomic nervous system. Lower SDNN points to high levels of
stress, fatigue, or potential cardiovascular strain.SDNN vs. RMSSD: While SDNN
reflects the overall variance (both branches of the autonomic nervous system),
RMSSD (Root Mean Square of Successive Differences) is preferred by consumer
wearables for measuring short-term, parasympathetic-driven changes, like
recovery.Normal Ranges and ContextTime Dependency: SDNN is best measured over 24
hours, but short-term (5-min) readings are common in wearable tech.Age Factor:
While heavily individual, average HRV can range between 40-70 ms, with higher
values generally found in younger individuals.24-Hour vs. Short-term: A 35ms
reading over 24 hours is different from a 35ms reading taken during a 5-minute
morning check.SDNN is best used to understand long-term trends in your overall
autonomic health and to track how your body is handling chronic stress.
