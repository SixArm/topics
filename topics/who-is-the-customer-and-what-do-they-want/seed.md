# Who is the customer and what do they want?

In Agile software development, identifying the "customer" depends on whether you are looking at who pays, who uses, or who represents their interests within the team. 

1. Who is the Customer?

Agile distinguishes between several types of "customers" to ensure the right value is delivered to the right people: 
- The Client (Paying Customer): The person or entity that orders the work and pays for the solution.
- The End User: The individual who actually uses the software to solve a problem or perform a task. In Agile, the end user is often considered the most critical "customer" because their satisfaction determines the product's success [25]. 
- The Product Owner (Proxy): Within the Scrum framework, the Product Owner (PO) acts as the "Voice of the Customer". They represent the interests of all stakeholders—including paying clients and end users—within the development team to ensure the team builds what is most valuable [11, 15, 18]. 
- Internal Stakeholders: These can include internal staff who run reports or support the application, who are also considered "customers" in certain contexts.

2. What Do They Want?

While specific needs vary by project, Agile customers generally seek outcomes over outputs. Their primary desires include: 

- Business Value: They want features that solve real problems, address pain points, or provide a competitive advantage. 
- Continuous Delivery & Early Value: Rather than waiting for a final "big bang" release, customers want usable software delivered in small, frequent increments so they can start seeing returns early [10, 13, 30]. 
- Collaboration and Engagement: A "great" Agile customer wants to be a partner, joining sprint reviews and trade-off discussions to ensure the product aligns with their vision [13, 20]. 
- Flexibility and Change: They want the ability to change their minds about priorities or details as they learn more during the development process. 
- Quality and Reliability: They demand software that is "faster, better, and cheaper," expecting high quality and a good user experience delivered on time. 

Tools for Identifying Needs

Agile teams use specific techniques to pin down exactly what customers want: 
- User Stories: Written in the format "As a persona, I want to, so that" to capture the "who, what, and why" of a requirement.
- User Personas: Fictional characters that help the team empathise with real users and identify their specific challenges and goals.
- User Story Mapping: A collaborative exercise used to visualise the customer's journey through the product and identify the "backbone" of the solution.
