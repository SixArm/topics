# Disciplined Agile principle 5: Choice is good

The fifth Disciplined Agile principle asserts that teams perform best when they can select their own practices and processes rather than following a single prescribed method. This principle builds directly on the ideas that context counts and pragmatism matters: if every team faces unique circumstances, then offering only one way to work guarantees a poor fit for most.

Traditional agile frameworks often mandate specific practices for every team. They require a particular backlog format, a specific meeting cadence, or a fixed set of roles. Disciplined Agile takes a fundamentally different approach by providing process goals paired with multiple strategies for achieving them. When a team needs to coordinate activities, for example, they can choose from daily stand-ups, electronic kanban boards, weekly status meetings, or other techniques based on what actually works in their environment.

Choice without understanding is just guessing. The Disciplined Agile toolkit addresses this by documenting the trade-offs associated with each option. Teams can evaluate which practices fit their current situation, team size, organizational constraints, and technical landscape. This turns selection from an uninformed gamble into a deliberate, informed decision.

The practical impact is significant. Teams avoid what practitioners call "framework prison," where rigid adherence to a single methodology prevents adaptation and improvement. Instead, teams evolve their Way of Working over time as they gain experience, as their context shifts, and as they discover which techniques deliver results. Choice keeps the process serving the team rather than the team serving the process.
