# Hero Treasure Dragon Quest (HTDQ)

The Hero-Treasure-Dragon-Quest is a leadership presentation framework that structures communication as a narrative with a clear logical hierarchy. It draws on the universal storytelling pattern of a hero pursuing a goal through obstacles, translating it into a method for organizing strategic arguments and recommendations.

The framework begins by identifying four core elements. The Hero is the protagonist, typically the team or organization taking action. The Treasure is the desired outcome or strategic objective. The Dragon is the primary obstacle or risk standing in the way. The Quest is the structured plan to overcome the obstacle and achieve the goal.

The presentation follows a pyramid structure. At the tip is the answer: a concise statement of what must be done, framed as the hero's mission to secure the treasure by neutralizing the dragon. Below the tip are supporting arguments that provide the logical foundation. These typically break into three pillars: preparation, which covers equipping for the specific threat; infiltration, which addresses navigating environmental and situational barriers; and execution, which details engaging the core challenge and extracting the value.

Each pillar then decomposes further into tactical specifics. Preparation covers offensive capabilities, defensive measures, and intelligence gathering. Navigation addresses entry points, known hazards, and contingency routes. Recovery focuses on containment of value, verification of outcomes, and post-completion logistics.

The strength of HTDQ is that it forces presenters to lead with the conclusion, support it with a small number of logical pillars, and ground each pillar in concrete actions. This top-down structure keeps executive audiences oriented on the strategic picture while providing enough depth to demonstrate rigor. It works especially well for proposals, initiative pitches, and risk-mitigation plans where both the goal and the obstacles need to be explicit.
