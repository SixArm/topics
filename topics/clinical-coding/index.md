# Clinical coding

Clinical coding is the process of translating medical diagnoses, procedures, and clinical documentation into standardized alphanumeric codes. The most widely used classification systems include ICD-10 (International Classification of Diseases) for diagnoses, CPT/OPCS-4 for procedures, and SNOMED CT for detailed clinical terminology. These codes create a structured, machine-readable representation of patient encounters.

Clinical coding serves several critical functions in healthcare systems. It drives reimbursement and payment models, as hospitals and providers submit coded claims to insurers and government payers. It enables population health tracking, epidemiological research, and public health surveillance by aggregating diagnostic data across institutions. It also supports hospital performance analysis, resource planning, and regulatory compliance.

The coding process requires trained professionals who review patient records, identify all relevant diagnoses including comorbidities, and map them to the correct codes following strict classification rules. Accuracy is essential because miscoding can result in claim denials, revenue loss, compliance violations, or corrupted research data. Coders work closely with clinicians to interpret complex or ambiguous documentation.

For technology professionals, clinical coding intersects with software development in areas such as electronic health record (EHR) systems, natural language processing pipelines that suggest codes from free-text notes, billing and revenue cycle management platforms, and clinical data warehouses. Understanding coding standards is necessary when building interoperability layers, designing FHIR-based APIs, or developing analytics tools that consume healthcare data.

Emerging applications of machine learning and large language models are automating parts of the coding workflow, particularly computer-assisted coding (CAC) systems that extract and recommend codes from clinical narratives. However, human review remains required for validation and edge cases where clinical judgment is needed.
