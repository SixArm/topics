# Agile swarming

Agile swarming is a collaborative technique where multiple team members, often the entire team, focus together on a single user story or task to drive it to completion as quickly as possible. It embodies the principle of "stop starting, start finishing," prioritizing throughput over individual busyness.

In traditional development, team members work in silos on separate tickets. Swarming reverses this by concentrating the team's collective effort on a specific bottleneck or high-priority item. A developer writes code while a tester simultaneously builds test scripts and another team member clears environmental hurdles or updates documentation. Everyone contributes based on their skills, working in parallel on the same goal.

Swarming is typically triggered when a high-priority story is blocked, or when the team recognizes excessive work in progress and needs to clear the board. The objective is to reduce cycle time, the duration for one item to move from start to finish, rather than to maximize the number of items in flight.

The benefits are significant. Swarming eliminates hand-off delays, such as a developer waiting for a tester to become available. It breaks down knowledge silos as junior members learn from seniors and developers gain insight into testing and operations. It reinforces collective ownership, where the team succeeds or fails together. It also prevents the "90% done" syndrome where many tasks are started but none are actually finished and usable.

Swarming differs from pairing in scale. Pairing involves two people on one task. Swarming involves the entire team or a large portion coordinating on one story until it meets the definition of done. Both techniques value collaboration, but swarming applies it at the team level.
