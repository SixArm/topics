# Agile-adjacent methodologies

Agile-adjacent methodologies are frameworks that share Agile's core values of iteration and customer feedback but solve specific problems where standard Scrum or Kanban fall short, such as long-term planning, deep user research, or business validation.

Lean Startup focuses on validated learning rather than software delivery. Its Build-Measure-Learn loop uses Minimum Viable Products to test business hypotheses with the least effort possible. It works best for early-stage teams seeking product-market fit rather than simply managing development velocity.

Shape Up, created by Basecamp, addresses sprint fatigue by replacing two-week cycles with six-week cycles followed by a two-week cool-down period for bug fixes and exploration. Senior members "shape" work by sketching rough boundaries before it reaches developers, ensuring feasibility without over-specifying. It suits teams that find Scrum too meeting-heavy or struggle to finish meaningful features within short sprints.

Design Thinking is a problem-solving framework that prioritizes human-centered design over technical requirements. Its five stages—Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test—focus on solving the right problem, whereas Agile focuses on solving the problem right. It excels during the discovery phase when the team has not yet confirmed what users actually need.

Jobs-to-be-Done views products through the lens of the "job" a customer is trying to accomplish. Customers do not buy products; they hire them to make progress in a specific circumstance. JTBD is best for redefining a product roadmap based on user motivation rather than a list of requested features.

These methodologies complement Agile by filling gaps in strategy, discovery, and work structure that iterative delivery alone does not address.
