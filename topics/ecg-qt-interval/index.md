# ECG QT interval

The ECG QT interval is a measurement on an electrocardiogram that represents the total time for the heart's ventricles to electrically activate and recover. It spans from the start of the QRS complex to the end of the T wave, capturing both ventricular depolarization and repolarization. In a healthy adult, the QT interval typically falls between 350 and 440 milliseconds, though it shortens naturally as heart rate increases.

Because the QT interval varies with heart rate, clinicians use a corrected value called QTc to standardize comparisons across patients. Common correction formulas, such as Bazett's formula, adjust the raw interval relative to the RR interval. A normal QTc is generally below 450 milliseconds for men and 460 to 470 milliseconds for women.

Abnormal prolongation of the QT interval is clinically significant because it increases the risk of dangerous arrhythmias, most notably Torsades de Pointes, a polymorphic ventricular tachycardia that can degenerate into ventricular fibrillation and sudden cardiac death. Long QT syndrome can be congenital, caused by inherited ion channel mutations, or acquired through medications such as certain antibiotics, antiarrhythmics, and antipsychotics. Electrolyte imbalances involving low potassium, calcium, or magnesium are also common triggers.

Short QT syndrome is a rarer condition where the interval is abnormally brief. It carries its own risk of sudden cardiac death due to increased susceptibility to atrial and ventricular fibrillation.

For technology professionals working in health monitoring systems, the QT interval is a critical parameter in ECG analysis algorithms. Automated QTc calculation, real-time prolongation alerts, and drug-interaction screening all depend on accurate detection of the QRS onset and T wave offset, making robust signal processing and waveform delineation essential components of any cardiac monitoring platform.
