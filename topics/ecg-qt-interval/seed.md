# ECG QT interval

The ECG QT interval is an electrocardiogram (ECG) measure representing the total time for the heart's ventricles to electrically contract and relax (depolarization and repolarization). It spans from the start of the QRS complex to the end of the T-wave, typically lasting 350-440 milliseconds, with faster heart rates shortening it.

Key Details About the QT Interval:

- Significance: It measures the duration of ventricular electrical activity. Abnormal prolongation (Long QT Syndrome) can trigger dangerous, even fatal, arrhythmias like Torsades de Pointes.
- Measurement: Calculated from the beginning of the QRS complex to the end of the T-wave.
- Corrected QT (QTc): Because the interval changes with heart rate, the QTc is used for accuracy. A normal QTc is generally below 450 ms for men and 460-470 ms for women.
- Causes of Prolongation: Common causes include medications (e.g., certain antibiotics, antipsychotics), electrolyte imbalances (low potassium, calcium, or magnesium), and genetic factors.
- Short QT Syndrome: Extremely short intervals (Short QT Syndrome) are rare but can also lead to sudden cardiac death.
