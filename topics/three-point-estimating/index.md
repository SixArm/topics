# Three-Point Estimating

Three-point estimating is a project management technique that improves the accuracy of time and cost forecasts by accounting for uncertainty. Instead of relying on a single best guess, you define three scenarios for each task: an optimistic estimate assuming everything goes perfectly, a most likely estimate based on typical conditions and past experience, and a pessimistic estimate assuming significant setbacks occur.

Two primary formulas combine these values into a single expected value. The triangular formula averages all three equally: (Optimistic + Most Likely + Pessimistic) / 3. This works well for quick estimates when you have limited data. The PERT formula weights the most likely outcome more heavily: (Optimistic + 4 x Most Likely + Pessimistic) / 6. PERT is the stronger choice when you have historical data or expert judgment, because it anchors the result closer to the realistic scenario while still incorporating the tails.

The spread between optimistic and pessimistic estimates reveals how much risk a task carries. Standard deviation is calculated as (Pessimistic - Optimistic) / 6. A large spread signals high uncertainty and flags the task for closer attention. A small spread indicates a predictable task that is unlikely to derail the schedule.

To apply three-point estimating, break the project into individual tasks, gather optimistic, most likely, and pessimistic values from experts or team members, calculate expected durations using your chosen formula, then use the results to build your project schedule and identify the critical path. The technique is particularly valuable during early planning when uncertainty is highest and single-point estimates are most likely to mislead.
