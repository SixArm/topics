# Disciplined Agile principle 7: Organize around products/services

Disciplined Agile's seventh principle calls for structuring teams and work around value streams rather than traditional functional silos. Instead of shuffling tasks between departments, organizations should build stable, long-lived teams aligned directly to the products and services that deliver value to customers. This principle reflects a fundamental shift in how enterprises think about organizational design: teams exist not to complete temporary assignments, but to continuously deliver and improve outcomes for the people they serve.

## Why traditional project-based structures fall short

Traditional project-based thinking treats work as temporary. A project starts, consumes its budget, and ends, often disbanding the team that built the expertise. When organizations organize around projects, they create a recurring cost: every new initiative requires assembling a team, establishing norms, building domain knowledge, and navigating new interpersonal dynamics. This cycle of forming and disbanding destroys institutional memory and forces repeated ramp-up costs.

Product-oriented thinking reverses this pattern entirely. Teams stay together, accumulate deep domain knowledge, and take ongoing responsibility for the quality and evolution of what they deliver. This continuity leads to better architectural decisions, faster onboarding, and stronger ownership. The team does not hand off its work at the end of a phase; it lives with the consequences of its decisions and has both the motivation and the context to improve continuously.

| Dimension | Project-based organization | Product-based organization |
|---|---|---|
| Team lifespan | Temporary, disbanded after delivery | Long-lived, stable membership |
| Knowledge retention | Lost when team dissolves | Accumulated over time |
| Ownership | Ambiguous, shared across departments | Clear, single team per product |
| Feedback loops | Slow, mediated by handoffs | Fast, within-team iteration |
| Funding model | Fixed budget, defined end date | Ongoing investment tied to value |
| Success metric | On time, on budget | Customer outcomes, product health |

## Cross-functional teams as the unit of delivery

The principle demands cross-functional teams. Rather than maintaining separate testing, development, and design departments, each product team includes everyone needed to deliver value end to end. This typically means:

- **Developers** who build and maintain the product codebase
- **Testers and quality engineers** who validate correctness and reliability
- **UX designers** who shape the user experience based on research and feedback
- **Business analysts** who translate customer needs into actionable requirements
- **Domain experts** who bring deep knowledge of the business context
- **Operations or DevOps specialists** who ensure the product runs reliably in production

This composition eliminates the handoffs and wait times that accumulate when work moves from silo to silo. A feature that once required coordination across four departments can move from concept to delivery within a single team. Dependencies shrink, communication overhead drops, and the team can make decisions at the speed of conversation rather than the speed of bureaucracy.

## Aligning teams to value streams

Organizations applying this principle identify their value streams, which are the end-to-end sequences of activities that produce outcomes customers care about, and align people directly to those streams. A value stream might be a customer-facing product, an internal platform, or a business service that a specific segment depends on.

This alignment ensures that team goals and organizational goals point in the same direction. When a team is responsible for a value stream, its priorities naturally reflect what matters to the customer and the business. There is no translation layer between "what the business needs" and "what the team works on."

Key steps in organizing around value streams include:

- **Mapping current value streams** to understand how value flows from idea to customer
- **Identifying bottlenecks and handoff points** that slow delivery or introduce errors
- **Forming teams around each stream** with the skills and authority to deliver independently
- **Empowering teams with decision rights** so they can respond to feedback without waiting for approval from outside the team
- **Establishing lightweight coordination mechanisms** for situations where multiple teams must collaborate on shared concerns

## Practical benefits of product-oriented organization

The practical result is reduced complexity across several dimensions. Teams spend less time coordinating across organizational boundaries and more time building. Feedback loops shorten because the people who design, build, test, and support a product sit together and share context continuously. Accountability becomes clear: one team owns one product, and success or failure is unambiguous.

| Benefit | How it manifests |
|---|---|
| Faster delivery | Fewer handoffs, less waiting, shorter cycle times |
| Higher quality | Team lives with its product and is motivated to maintain it well |
| Clearer accountability | No ambiguity about who owns what |
| Better morale | Stable teams build trust, psychological safety, and shared purpose |
| Easier scaling | New products or services get new teams rather than overloading existing silos |
| Improved customer focus | Team goals map directly to customer outcomes |

## Challenges and considerations

Adopting this principle is not without friction. Organizations transitioning from functional silos often encounter resistance from managers whose authority is tied to department boundaries. Specialists may worry about career growth when embedded in product teams rather than communities of practice within their discipline.

Successful transitions address these concerns head-on:

- **Communities of practice** allow specialists to maintain professional growth, share techniques, and establish standards without reverting to siloed departments
- **Leadership reorientation** shifts managers from directing work to enabling teams, removing impediments, and developing people
- **Gradual migration** allows organizations to pilot the product-team model on one or two value streams before reorganizing wholesale
- **Shared services teams** handle genuinely cross-cutting concerns like infrastructure, security, or compliance without becoming bottlenecks

The goal is not to eliminate all specialization or coordination, but to make the product team the primary organizational unit and to treat everything else as support for that team's ability to deliver value.

## The organizational structure as an enabler

Organizing around products and services turns the organizational structure itself into an enabler of agility rather than an obstacle to it. Conway's Law observes that systems tend to mirror the communication structures of the organizations that build them. When teams are organized by function, the products they build tend to reflect those functional boundaries, with seams, inconsistencies, and integration gaps. When teams are organized around products, the systems they build tend to be more cohesive, better integrated, and more aligned to user needs.

This principle is not merely a structural recommendation. It is a strategic choice that shapes culture, incentives, communication patterns, and ultimately the quality of what an organization delivers. Getting the organizational design right creates the conditions in which all the other Disciplined Agile principles can operate effectively.

## Related

Teams applying this principle benefit from understanding the other Disciplined Agile principles, particularly Principle 1 (Delight Customers) and Principle 6 (Optimize Flow), which reinforce the customer-centric and flow-oriented thinking that product-based organization enables. Lean portfolio management, value stream mapping, and Team Topologies by Skelton and Pais provide complementary frameworks for designing effective product-aligned organizations. Practitioners should also explore related concepts such as DevOps culture, continuous delivery, and platform engineering, all of which are natural extensions of organizing teams around what they deliver.

## Summary

Disciplined Agile Principle 7 directs organizations to structure themselves around the products and services that deliver value to customers, rather than around temporary projects or functional specialties. By forming stable, cross-functional teams aligned to value streams, organizations reduce coordination overhead, shorten feedback loops, strengthen ownership, and create the structural conditions for sustained agility. The shift from project thinking to product thinking is one of the most consequential organizational design decisions an enterprise can make, and getting it right amplifies the effectiveness of every other agile practice.
