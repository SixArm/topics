# OKR quotations: challenges

"There are many dimensions of valuable work: no security holes, being nice, not
reinventing the wheel, documenting well, stability, refactorability, code being
reusable by other teams, and more. OKRs can't cover all those facets."

"I felt a sort of a weird disconnect between OKRs and actual work/performance.
On the one hand, OKRs were presumably critically important, but on the other,
they weren't directly tied to individual performance. I still have trouble
wrapping my head around that."

"I found that as a team lead, to the extent that OKRs were stressed, any non-OKR
related work became highly disincentivized. Refactor? Write more integration
tests? Hell no, not if it doesn't directly impact OKRs. We had stories in the
backlog that really should have been done because they would have helped other
teams and yielded a positive return, but anything non-OKR was just dropped on
the floor."

"It can be tough and time consuming to get the measurements in place necessary
to gauge the results. We pro-actively get data collection in place for reasons
that include helping to launch and score OKRs."

"Every time I've seen OKRs tried (over and over again in the span of a 30-year
career), the "goals" are based on whatever the priority/flavor-of-the month
happens to be when goal-setting is announced. I've never seen those priorities
last an entire year, but I have been called to account for why I didn't
personally meet the goals that I was pressured into setting "for myself" even
though the priorities shifted over the course of the year."

"OKRs causing slowdowns is a real issue, but is actually a problem of management
and company as a whole. Teams want to be silo-ed and not bothered by anyone
else, hence set their goals and just do them, while upper management never sets
some actual objectives and roadmap which could have been mapped into cross team
projects and goals. When goals become a religious quarter to quarter thing
without allowing any deviation, then everything is slow."

"People who get stuff done do so in spite of OKRs or other formalisms.
OKRs do not help orient work towards what matters, maintain accountability,
alert management to schedule slippage, or unify team efforts."

"We do quarterly OKRs. Quarterly deadlines are completely arbitrary and my
actual deadlines aren't tied to the quarters. So I (and everyone around me)
don't take them seriously. We take the actual deadlines seriously. The only
thing OKRs seem to do is provide direction to the higher ups about what my team
is up to."

"Refactoring is a really tough sell for an OKR. We use OKRs and we have had a
couple quality initiatives in the past that were tied directly to OKRs and even
then it’s hard to get that work prioritized. It’s hard to come up with a key
result that can be measured once the work is done — other than “it still works
the same”."

"Non-SMART OKRs anywhere in the chain allow opportunistic employees to spin
tales about how they're doing great regardless of actual results."

"If the executive team does not publish company-wide objectives in time for
downstream teams to build their own objectives upon them, then the downstream
teams don't have the guidance they need to fit the higher objectives."

"A lot of organizational time is spent coming up with OKRs only for them to be
either overridden due to the reality of running a business or just by
CXOs/VPs/Directors deciding that something else requires the teams focus."

"Emphasis on these metrics and their associated processes is an important part
of a process driven organization, where you want everything to be consistent,
and are willing to sacrifice some efficiency (and some job satisfaction), while
carrying an admin layer. This is probably true for most big companies."

"The fundamental issue is that in almost all larger companies, upper management
does not trust that their employees are intrinsically motivated to do a good
job, and smart enough to determine what "a good job" is. So rather than having a
chain of trust from upper management to middle management to individual
contributors, they seek to create a measurable control system. This replaces
people's intrinsic motivation with extrinsic motivation, where most people are
trying to make their numbers look good."

"You need good executives, and you need to be able to depend on executives who
have good guts. You cannot automate your way into being successful, at the end
of it all running a company is still pretty much a social endeavour, one that
cannot be partitioned down to individual units."

"As an individual engineer, you are not given organizational authority, budgets,
or agency beyond your line of work. You literally haven't been given the tools
to own metrics and OKRs. So why would you care?"

"To use an analogy: One team raises horses and decides their OKR is 10% faster.
One team builds buggies and decides their OKR is 10% lighter. If the teams ever
talked to actual customers, they'd find that people wants cars, not horse and
carriage.
"
