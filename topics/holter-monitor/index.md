# Holter monitor

A Holter monitor is a small, wearable device that continuously records the heart's electrical activity, known as an electrocardiogram (ECG), typically over a period of 24 to 48 hours or longer. It is designed to detect irregular heartbeats, called arrhythmias, that a standard short-duration ECG performed in a clinical setting might miss. Physicians use it to investigate symptoms such as heart palpitations, dizziness, and unexplained fainting episodes.

The device works by connecting to a set of electrodes, which are small adhesive patches placed on the patient's chest. These electrodes feed continuous signal data to a portable recorder that the patient carries or wears, usually clipped to a belt or placed in a pocket. The recorder stores heart rate and rhythm data for later analysis. During the monitoring period, the patient goes about normal daily activities, wearing the device under clothing. Bathing, showering, and swimming are typically not permitted because the device and electrodes must remain dry.

Patients are generally asked to maintain a written diary during the monitoring period, logging activities and any symptoms they experience along with the exact times of occurrence. This diary allows the physician to correlate reported symptoms with the recorded ECG data, making it easier to identify whether a particular arrhythmia coincides with a clinical event.

After the monitoring period, the patient returns the device. The physician or a technician then reviews the accumulated data to determine the cause of symptoms, assess the effectiveness of heart medications or treatments, and check for abnormal heart rhythms that may require intervention.

Wearing a Holter monitor is painless and safe, making it a practical diagnostic tool for ambulatory cardiac monitoring in everyday conditions.
