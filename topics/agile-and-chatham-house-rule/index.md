# Agile and the Chatham House Rule

Agile teams thrive on open, honest communication. Retrospectives, planning sessions, and cross-functional workshops all depend on participants sharing candid feedback without fear of blame or professional consequences. The Chatham House Rule provides a structured way to create that psychological safety, particularly in contexts where sensitivity, competition, or regulatory concerns might otherwise silence important voices.

The Chatham House Rule states that participants may use information shared in a meeting, but may not reveal the identity or affiliation of any speaker. This simple agreement unlocks frank discussion among people who might otherwise hedge their words due to corporate policy, competitive dynamics, or political exposure.

The combination proves especially valuable in several scenarios. When technical experts, regulators, and industry leaders convene to discuss fast-moving areas such as AI governance, cybersecurity, or compliance frameworks, the rule encourages participants to share real-world failures and systemic weaknesses rather than offering sanitized talking points. In software development communities, practitioners have used the rule during retreats and unconferences to debate controversial topics like the future of test-driven development or the impact of generative AI on engineering practices.

Organizations also apply the pairing when running agile policy-making workshops, where iterative, stakeholder-driven processes benefit from participants who feel free to challenge assumptions and surface inconvenient truths. Government and defense programs have adopted similar principles for evidence-based strategy development conducted in closed sessions.

For technology professionals, the practical takeaway is straightforward: when facilitating sensitive discussions that require the same openness expected in agile ceremonies, explicitly adopting the Chatham House Rule removes attribution risk and produces more honest, actionable outcomes.
