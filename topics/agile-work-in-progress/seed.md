# Agile work-in-progress (WIP)

In Agile, work-in-progress (WIP) refers to any task that has been started but is not yet finished. It represents "inventory" in your workflow—work that is consuming time and energy but has not yet delivered value to the customer. 

## The WIP Limit

In frameworks like Kanban, teams set WIP Limits: a fixed cap on the number of items allowed in a specific stage of the workflow (e.g., "only 3 tasks in Testing at a time"). 

## Why Limiting WIP is Critical

Reduces Context Switching: When team members juggle too many tasks, they lose time "re-learning" where they left off. Focus leads to faster completion. 

Exposes Bottlenecks: If your "In Progress" column is empty but "Testing" is at its limit, you immediately see where the flow is blocked. 

Shortens Lead Time: According to Little’s Law, the more items you have in progress, the longer it takes for any single item to get done. 

Improves Quality: By focusing on one thing at a time, developers are less likely to make "hurry-up" mistakes. 

## "Stop Starting, Start Finishing"

This is the mantra of WIP management. Instead of starting a new task because you're blocked on another, the goal is to resolve the blockage on the current task first. 

## How to Calculate WIP

Simply count the cards currently in the "Active" columns of your board (excluding "To Do" and "Done"). 

- Too much WIP: High stress, slow delivery, "stale" code.
- Too little WIP: Idle team members (though this is rare). 

## Common WIP Signals

- The "Handoff" Pile-up: A massive stack of items waiting for QA usually means your WIP limit for "Development" is too high compared to your testing capacity.
- The "Ghost" WIP: Tasks that aren't on the board but people are working on anyway. This "dark work" destroys predictability.
