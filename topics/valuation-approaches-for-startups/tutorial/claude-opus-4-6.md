# Valuation approaches for startups

## Introduction

Startup valuation is the process of estimating the current or potential value of a startup company. For technology professionals involved in founding, joining, or investing in startups, understanding valuation is essential. Valuation determines how much equity founders give up in exchange for funding, how stock options are priced, and how investors assess risk versus reward. Unlike valuing a mature company with years of financial history, startup valuation must contend with limited data, unproven markets, and speculative revenue projections. Several established approaches exist, each suited to different stages and circumstances.

## Why Valuation Matters for Technology Professionals

Valuation is not just a concern for finance teams. Technology professionals encounter it when negotiating equity compensation, evaluating job offers at startups, participating in fundraising, or making angel investments. A working knowledge of valuation approaches helps you assess whether an offer is fair, whether an investment thesis holds up, and whether a company's fundraising narrative aligns with financial reality.

- **Equity compensation**: Stock options and restricted stock are priced relative to the company's valuation. Understanding valuation helps you evaluate the real worth of an equity package.
- **Fundraising participation**: Engineers and technical co-founders often participate in pitch meetings and due diligence. Knowing how investors think about value strengthens your position.
- **Career decisions**: Joining a startup at a $10M valuation versus a $500M valuation carries different risk-reward profiles. Valuation literacy helps you make informed choices.
- **409A compliance**: U.S. startups must conduct independent 409A valuations to set the fair market value of common stock for tax purposes.

## Market Approach

The market approach estimates a startup's value by comparing it to similar companies that have been recently valued, acquired, or publicly listed. It relies on comparable sales, industry benchmarks, and market multiples such as revenue multiples or EBITDA multiples.

This approach works best when reliable comparables exist. For a SaaS company, for example, you might look at recent acquisitions of similar SaaS companies and apply the same revenue multiple. The challenge is that truly comparable startups are rare, especially for companies pioneering new markets or technologies.

| Strength | Limitation |
|---|---|
| Grounded in real market data | Difficult to find truly comparable companies |
| Intuitive and widely understood | Market conditions change rapidly |
| Useful when revenue exists | Less applicable to pre-revenue startups |
| Benchmarks are publicly available for some sectors | Comparables may reflect irrational market exuberance |

## Income Approach

The income approach values a startup based on its projected future earnings and cash flows. The most common technique is **discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis**, which estimates expected cash flows over a future period and discounts them back to present value using an appropriate discount rate. A related technique is the **capitalization of earnings model**, which divides expected earnings by a capitalization rate.

The income approach is often considered the most rigorous method because it focuses on the fundamental financial performance of the business. However, it demands accurate forecasting, which is inherently difficult for early-stage companies with limited operating history. Small changes in assumptions about growth rates or discount rates can produce wildly different valuations.

- **Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)**: Projects cash flows for 5-10 years, applies a terminal value, and discounts everything to present value. Requires assumptions about revenue growth, margins, capital expenditures, and the weighted average cost of capital.
- **Capitalization of Earnings**: Simpler than DCF, this method divides a single estimate of future earnings by a capitalization rate. Best suited for companies with stable, predictable earnings.
- **Discount rate selection**: Early-stage startups typically use high discount rates (30-60%) to reflect the elevated risk, which heavily reduces the present value of distant cash flows.

## Scorecard Method

The scorecard method is a hybrid approach commonly used for early-stage startups that lack significant revenue or market data. It begins with the average pre-money valuation for comparable startups in the same region and stage, then adjusts that baseline up or down based on a weighted scorecard of qualitative factors.

Typical scorecard factors and their weightings include:

| Factor | Typical Weight |
|---|---|
| Strength of the management team | 30% |
| Size of the market opportunity | 25% |
| Product or technology | 15% |
| Competitive environment | 10% |
| Marketing and sales channels | 10% |
| Need for additional investment | 5% |
| Other factors | 5% |

Each factor is rated relative to the baseline (e.g., 150% if the team is exceptionally strong, 75% if the market is smaller than average). The weighted sum of these percentages is applied to the average valuation to produce the startup's estimated value.

The scorecard method is practical because it accommodates judgment and qualitative assessment while still producing a structured, defensible number. It is especially popular among angel investor groups.

## Berkus Method

The Berkus method, developed by angel investor Dave Berkus, assigns a dollar value to each of five key risk factors. It is designed specifically for pre-revenue startups and avoids the need for financial projections entirely.

The five factors are:

- **Sound idea (basic value)**: Does the concept address a real problem with a viable solution?
- **Prototype (reducing technology risk)**: Has the team built a working prototype or proof of concept?
- **Quality management team (reducing execution risk)**: Does the team have the skills and experience to execute?
- **Strategic relationships (reducing market risk)**: Are there partnerships, advisors, or customer commitments that de-risk the go-to-market?
- **Product rollout or sales (reducing production risk)**: Has the product launched or generated early sales?

Each factor can be assigned up to a maximum value (often $500K per factor in the original formulation, though this ceiling has been adjusted upward in some markets). The total across all five factors yields the pre-money valuation.

| Factor | Maximum Value (Original) | Risk Addressed |
|---|---|---|
| Sound idea | $500K | Concept risk |
| Prototype | $500K | Technology risk |
| Quality management team | $500K | Execution risk |
| Strategic relationships | $500K | Market risk |
| Product rollout or sales | $500K | Production risk |
| **Maximum total** | **$2.5M** | |

The Berkus method is deliberately simple. It works best as a quick sanity check or starting point for negotiation rather than as a precise valuation tool.

## Choosing the Right Approach

No single valuation method is universally correct. The appropriate approach depends on the startup's stage, available data, and the context of the valuation.

| Stage | Recommended Approaches | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-revenue / idea stage | Berkus, Scorecard | No financial data to model; qualitative factors dominate |
| Early revenue | Scorecard, Market approach | Some comparables become available; revenue provides an anchor |
| Growth stage | Market approach, Income approach | Revenue multiples are meaningful; cash flow projections become feasible |
| Late stage / pre-IPO | Income approach, Market approach | Robust financials allow DCF analysis; public market comparables are relevant |

In practice, investors and founders often use multiple methods and triangulate the results. A valuation supported by two or three independent approaches carries more credibility than one derived from a single method.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Over-reliance on comparable companies**: Market multiples from boom periods can produce inflated valuations that don't hold up in downturns.
- **Optimistic projections**: Founders tend to overestimate revenue growth and underestimate costs. Investors scrutinize assumptions carefully.
- **Ignoring dilution**: Valuation is only meaningful in the context of the full capitalization table, including option pools and convertible instruments.
- **Confusing pre-money and post-money**: Pre-money valuation is the value before new investment; post-money is after. Mixing these up changes the equity math significantly.
- **Neglecting stage-appropriate methods**: Applying DCF analysis to a pre-revenue startup with no operating history produces a false sense of precision.

## Summary

Startup valuation combines quantitative analysis with qualitative judgment, and the right approach depends on the company's maturity and available data. The market approach benchmarks against comparable companies, the income approach projects and discounts future cash flows, the scorecard method applies weighted qualitative factors to a baseline valuation, and the Berkus method assigns dollar values to key risk factors for pre-revenue startups. Technology professionals benefit from understanding all four approaches because valuation directly affects equity compensation, investment decisions, and career choices. The most credible valuations use multiple methods to triangulate a defensible range rather than relying on any single technique.
