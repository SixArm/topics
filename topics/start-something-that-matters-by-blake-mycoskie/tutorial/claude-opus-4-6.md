# "Start Something That Matters" by Blake Mycoskie

"Start Something That Matters" is a book by Blake Mycoskie, the founder of TOMS Shoes, published in 2011. The book combines memoir with practical entrepreneurship advice, centered on the idea that businesses can be vehicles for social change. Mycoskie shares the origin story of TOMS and its pioneering "one for one" model, then distills his experience into actionable guidance for technology professionals, social entrepreneurs, and anyone seeking to build a purpose-driven venture. The book has become a foundational text in the social enterprise movement and remains relevant as companies increasingly integrate mission and margin.

## The Origin of TOMS and the "One for One" Model

The book opens with Mycoskie's account of traveling to Argentina in 2006, where he witnessed children growing up without shoes and the health and educational consequences that followed. Rather than launching a traditional charity, he conceived a for-profit company that would donate a pair of shoes for every pair sold. This "one for one" model was radical at the time because it embedded philanthropy directly into the revenue engine of the business, making giving sustainable rather than dependent on donations. TOMS grew from a small operation selling Argentine alpargata-style shoes out of Mycoskie's apartment to a global brand, demonstrating that consumers would actively choose products tied to a social mission. For technology professionals, the TOMS story illustrates how a simple, compelling value proposition, clearly communicated, can drive viral growth without massive marketing budgets.

## Core Principles of the Book

Mycoskie organizes his advice around six core principles that he considers essential for starting something that matters. These principles apply broadly, whether you are building a SaaS platform, an open-source project, or a hardware startup with a social mission.

| Principle | Description | Relevance for Technology Professionals |
|---|---|---|
| Find your story | Identify a compelling narrative that connects your personal experience to the problem you solve. | A strong founder story differentiates a product in crowded markets and builds investor confidence. |
| Face your fears | Acknowledge the risks of starting something new and act despite uncertainty. | Technology ventures involve technical risk, market risk, and execution risk simultaneously. |
| Be resourceful without resources | Bootstrap creatively, leveraging constraints as a forcing function for innovation. | Lean startup methodology and MVP thinking echo this principle directly. |
| Keep it simple | Strip away complexity in your product, messaging, and operations. | Simplicity in product design, architecture, and user experience drives adoption. |
| Build trust | Operate with transparency and authenticity to earn lasting loyalty from customers and partners. | Trust is the foundation of developer communities, open-source ecosystems, and enterprise sales. |
| Giving is good business | Integrate social impact into your business model rather than treating it as an afterthought. | Corporate social responsibility and ESG considerations increasingly influence hiring, retention, and customer choice in technology. |

## Finding Your Story

Mycoskie argues that every meaningful venture begins with a personal story that connects the founder to the problem. He does not mean a polished marketing narrative; he means an authentic account of why you care. In technology, this translates to understanding the pain point you are solving from direct experience. Founders who have personally struggled with the problem they address tend to build better products because they have deep empathy for their users. Mycoskie encourages aspiring entrepreneurs to reflect on moments in their lives that revealed injustice, inefficiency, or unmet need, and to use those moments as the foundation for a venture.

## Facing Fears and Taking Action

A significant portion of the book addresses the psychological barriers to starting a venture. Mycoskie recounts his own doubts, from questioning whether anyone would buy shoes from an unknown brand to worrying about the logistics of shoe donations in developing countries. His core advice is that waiting for perfect conditions guarantees inaction. For technology professionals accustomed to engineering certainty, this is a critical mindset shift. Shipping an imperfect product and iterating based on real feedback is almost always superior to prolonged planning. Mycoskie's experience validates the lean startup principle of building, measuring, and learning in rapid cycles.

## Being Resourceful Without Resources

Mycoskie started TOMS with no shoe industry experience, no factory relationships, and limited capital. He leveraged personal networks, bartered for services, and found creative ways to generate publicity without a marketing budget. This chapter resonates strongly with bootstrapped technology startups and open-source projects. The key lessons include:

- Constraints force creativity and often lead to more elegant solutions than unlimited budgets produce.
- Early customers and community members can become your most effective marketing channel if you genuinely solve their problems.
- Partnerships with complementary organizations can substitute for capital-intensive capabilities you cannot yet build in-house.
- Saying no to opportunities that do not align with your core mission preserves focus and resources for what matters most.

## Simplicity as a Strategic Advantage

Mycoskie emphasizes that TOMS succeeded in part because the value proposition was immediately understandable: buy a pair of shoes, and a child in need gets a pair. There was no complex explanation required. For technology professionals, this principle applies at multiple levels:

- **Product design**: Features should solve clear problems. Feature bloat erodes usability and increases maintenance burden.
- **Messaging**: If you cannot explain your product's value in one sentence, your positioning needs work.
- **Architecture**: Simple systems are easier to debug, scale, and hand off to new team members.
- **Business model**: Revenue models that customers can understand without a spreadsheet generate less friction in the sales process.

## Building Trust Through Transparency

Mycoskie dedicates significant attention to the idea that trust is a competitive advantage. TOMS published detailed reports on its giving programs, invited journalists and customers on "giving trips" to see donations firsthand, and maintained open communication about challenges. In the technology industry, trust manifests in several ways:

- **Open-source contributions**: Companies that contribute to the commons build goodwill and attract talent.
- **Security and privacy practices**: Transparent data handling policies differentiate products in an era of increasing regulation and consumer awareness.
- **Honest communication**: Acknowledging outages, bugs, and limitations publicly builds more credibility than spin.
- **Community engagement**: Actively listening to user feedback and visibly incorporating it demonstrates respect for your user base.

## Giving as a Business Strategy

The book's central thesis is that giving is not a cost center but a growth driver. Mycoskie presents evidence that the social mission of TOMS generated earned media coverage worth millions, attracted employees willing to work for below-market compensation, and created a passionate customer base that evangelized the brand without incentives. For technology companies, integrating purpose into the business model can yield measurable advantages:

| Business Benefit | How Purpose Drives It |
|---|---|
| Talent acquisition | Mission-driven companies attract candidates who prioritize meaning alongside compensation. |
| Customer retention | Customers who share your values exhibit higher lifetime value and lower churn. |
| Brand differentiation | A genuine social mission is difficult for competitors to replicate authentically. |
| Media and public relations | Purpose-driven stories are inherently more shareable and newsworthy. |
| Employee engagement | Teams aligned around a shared mission demonstrate higher productivity and lower turnover. |

## Criticisms and Limitations

No discussion of the book is complete without acknowledging the criticisms that have been raised about the TOMS model. Development economists have pointed out that donating goods to developing countries can undermine local industries, create dependency, and fail to address root causes of poverty. TOMS itself evolved its model over time, shifting from pure shoe donations to investing in local manufacturing, health programs, and education initiatives. For technology professionals, these criticisms offer important lessons:

- Good intentions do not guarantee good outcomes. Measure the impact of your social initiatives rigorously.
- Systems thinking matters. A well-intentioned intervention can have unintended consequences in complex systems.
- Iterating on your social model is just as important as iterating on your product. Be willing to change your approach based on evidence.

## Applying the Book's Lessons in Technology

Technology professionals can apply Mycoskie's framework in several practical ways, even if they are not building a social enterprise:

- **Side projects with purpose**: Use your technical skills to build tools that address problems in underserved communities, contributing to digital inclusion and access.
- **Ethical product development**: Embed ethical considerations into product decisions from the outset, rather than bolting them on after launch.
- **Mentorship and knowledge sharing**: Giving your time and expertise to emerging professionals creates compounding returns for the industry.
- **Sustainable business models**: Design revenue models that align your financial incentives with positive outcomes for users, rather than extracting value through dark patterns or exploitative data practices.
- **Stakeholder thinking**: Consider the impact of your product on all stakeholders, including users, employees, communities, and the environment, not just shareholders.

## Related

Readers interested in deepening their understanding of purpose-driven business should explore topics such as the business model canvas for structuring social enterprises, corporate social responsibility frameworks, the B Corporation certification and its standards, the lean startup methodology for validating social impact hypotheses, design thinking for human-centered problem solving, and books like "Crossing the Chasm" by Geoffrey Moore for understanding how mission-driven products reach mainstream markets. The broader literature on social entrepreneurship, stakeholder capitalism, and environmental impact provides essential context for applying Mycoskie's ideas at scale.

## Summary

"Start Something That Matters" by Blake Mycoskie is both a memoir and a practical guide that argues businesses can and should be engines for social good. Through the story of TOMS Shoes and its "one for one" model, Mycoskie demonstrates that finding your story, facing fears, being resourceful, keeping things simple, building trust, and integrating giving into your business model are not just idealistic aspirations but actionable strategies that drive growth, attract talent, and build lasting brands. While the TOMS model has faced legitimate criticism regarding its development impact, the book's core message remains powerful for technology professionals: aligning your work with a purpose larger than profit creates sustainable competitive advantages and a more fulfilling career. The key takeaway is that starting something that matters does not require choosing between doing well and doing good.
