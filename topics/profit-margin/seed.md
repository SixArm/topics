# Profit margin

Profit Margin is the metric that tells you how much of every pound earned actually stays in your pocket. While unit economics looks at the customer, profit margin looks at the whole machine.

## 1. The Two Main Types

* Gross Profit Margin: Measures production efficiency.
* Formula: $\frac{\text{Revenue} - \text{COGS}}{\text{Revenue}}$
   * (COGS = Cost of Goods Sold: raw materials, direct labor).
* Net Profit Margin: Measures total business health (the "bottom line").
* Formula: $\frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Revenue}}$
   * This includes everything: rent, taxes, interest, and marketing.

## 2. The Margin vs. Volume Trade-off
Using the Marginal Return logic we discussed, businesses often face a choice:

* High Margin / Low Volume: Luxury goods (e.g., Rolex). You make a massive profit on every unit, so you don't need to sell many.
* Low Margin / High Volume: Commodities (e.g., Amazon or Supermarkets). You make pennies per unit but sell millions of units.

------------------------------
## 3. Profit Margin as a "Driver"
If your goal is to increase Total Profit, your Driver Tree reveals three ways to "expand" your margin:

   1. Price (The Lever): Raising prices increases margin directly (if volume holds).
   2. Variable Costs (The Efficiency): Reducing the cost of materials or shipping (improving Gross Margin).
   3. Fixed Costs (The Scale): Spreading rent and salaries across more units (improving Net Margin).
