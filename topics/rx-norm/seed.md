# RxNorm

RxNorm is a standardised nomenclature for clinical drugs and a semantic interoperability tool produced by the [U.S. National Library of Medicine (NLM)](https://www.nlm.nih.gov/research/umls/rxnorm/index.html).  
Its primary purpose is to allow computer systems to communicate drug-related information unambiguously by grouping different names for the same drug into a single "normalised" concept.

## Key Features

- Normalised Names: It creates a standard name for every drug based on its active ingredients, strength, and dose form (e.g., "Naproxen 250 MG Oral Tablet").
- RXCUI: Every normalised concept is assigned a unique identifier called an RxNorm Concept Unique Identifier (RXCUI).
- Mapping & Linking: It links these standard names to various proprietary drug vocabularies (like Micromedex or First Databank) and the FDA’s National Drug Codes (NDCs).
- Scope: It covers most prescription and over-the-counter drugs available in the United States.

## Why It Matters

In healthcare, different systems (pharmacies, hospitals, insurance providers) often use different naming conventions for the same medication. RxNorm acts as a "universal translator," ensuring that when one system records a prescription, another system understands exactly what it is, which is critical for [patient safety and reducing medication errors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RxNorm).
