# Definition of Customer (DoC)

The customer is the person or entity that pays for the system, owns the business requirements, and ultimately decides if the product is a success. While customers are often confused with end users, the customer is specifically the one who authorizes the work and realizes the business value.

The customer acts as the buyer who provides funding and resources, the decision maker who has the final say on whether a feature is valuable enough to build, and the visionary who defines overarching business goals and the reason the project exists.

The distinction between customer and end user matters in practice. The customer wants return on investment, competitive advantage, and project completion. For example, a hospital administrator buying a new patient portal is the customer. The end user wants ease of use, speed, and specific functionality. For example, the nurses and doctors using that portal daily are the end users. Conflating these roles leads to building software that satisfies neither party well.

In the software development lifecycle, the customer typically operates through a Product Owner who acts as their proxy. Because a high-level customer such as a CEO rarely has time for development team meetings, the Product Owner translates their business vision into a prioritized backlog. Only the customer or their proxy can officially accept a completed user story as meeting the business requirement. Customers participate in feedback loops through demonstrations, weekly showcases, or ongoing research deployments, where they see working software and provide direction for continued development.
