# Customer Lifetime Value : Customer Acquisition Cost (CLV:CAC)

The CLV:CAC ratio measures the relationship between the total value a customer generates over their lifetime and the cost required to acquire that customer. It is the core efficiency metric for any growth-oriented business: for every dollar spent on sales and marketing, how many dollars of long-term profit come back?

Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) is the total net profit a customer produces before they churn. Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) is total sales and marketing spend divided by the number of new customers acquired in that period. The ratio is simply CLV divided by CAC.

Benchmarks are well established. A ratio of 1:1 or lower means the business loses money on every customer, and scaling accelerates the loss. A ratio of 2:1 covers costs but leaves little margin for error. The widely accepted target is 3:1, indicating a sustainable and investable growth engine, particularly in SaaS. A ratio above 5:1 suggests the company may be under-investing in growth and leaving market share on the table.

The ratio alone is incomplete without the payback period, which measures how quickly a customer's revenue recoups their acquisition cost. A business can show a strong 5:1 ratio yet run out of cash if payback takes several years. Healthy startups typically target payback within twelve months.

Improving the ratio comes down to two levers. Raise CLV by increasing prices, expanding revenue per customer through upsells, or reducing churn so customers stay longer. Lower CAC by improving conversion rates, sourcing cheaper leads, or driving organic acquisition through word-of-mouth and product-led growth. The strongest businesses work both levers simultaneously.
