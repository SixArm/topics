# Electrocardiogram vs photoplethysmogram

An electrocardiogram (ECG) and a photoplethysmogram (PPG) both measure cardiac activity, but they rely on fundamentally different sensing mechanisms and serve different clinical roles.

An ECG uses electrodes placed on the body to measure the electrical activity of the heart. It captures both atrial and ventricular activity, requires at least two electrodes to form a closed loop, and needs dedicated hardware such as Holter monitors or handheld devices. ECG is the gold standard for heart rate and rhythm assessment and can be directly diagnostic.

A PPG uses a light source and photodetector to measure changes in capillary blood volume, reflecting the mechanical activity of the arterial blood pressure waveform. It captures only ventricular activity and requires just a single contact point. PPG signals can be collected with consumer hardware such as smartphones and smartwatches, making it far more accessible for widespread screening and long-term continuous monitoring.

Despite their different origins, both signals are directly linked to the cardiac cycle. Studies comparing peak-to-peak intervals from PPG with RR intervals from simultaneously recorded ECG data have reported a 99.3% correlation, demonstrating that PPG can accurately determine heart rate and pulse rhythm regularity. This makes PPG effective for detecting cardiac irregularities such as atrial fibrillation, even though PPG waveforms lack the P-wave information visible on ECG.

One notable difference is timing: the PPG waveform is delayed relative to the ECG signal due to pulse arrival time, the interval required for the pressure wave from ventricular contraction to travel through the arterial system to the sensor location.

Currently, PPG-based detection still requires a confirmatory ECG for diagnosis in patients without a prior atrial fibrillation diagnosis. ECG remains authoritative for rhythm confirmation, with clinical oversight required in both cases.
